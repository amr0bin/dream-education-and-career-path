Phase One · Now to Year 5 · Building the Bridge

01 · Field fit
When I read deeply about the global mental health treatment gap — the 75% of people with depression and anxiety in low-income countries who receive no care at all — does something in me respond to that as my problem to work on, or do I feel compassion from a distance?

The distinction between genuine pull and intellectual interest matters. One sustains a twenty-year reorientation. The other doesn't.

02 · Entry point
Is the right next job one that anchors me in the Canadian health or research sector specifically — or am I willing to consider roles at organizations with explicit international mandates, even if they require different kinds of flexibility?

Organizations like the International Development Research Centre, global health NGOs with Canadian offices, or health authorities with research partnerships abroad represent a different kind of opportunity than domestic higher education.

03 · Literacy building
After reading the 2018 Lancet Commission on Global Mental Health and Vikram Patel's Where There Is No Psychiatrist, do I feel drawn further in — or do I feel I was right to keep this at the periphery of my awareness?

These two texts are the entry point to the field's current thinking. The honest response to them is better information than any strategic plan.

04 · Quantitative appetite
When I engage with epidemiological methods — DALYs, burden-of-disease calculations, health systems data — do I find I can develop genuine fluency there, or does that mode of knowing feel fundamentally foreign to how I think?

The LSHTM short courses in epidemiology and statistics are the cheapest possible test of this. Take them before changing any degree plans.

05 · MSc decision
Which credential serves the life I actually want more: the Leeds MSc in Disability Studies, which deepens a justice framework I've already begun building — or the LSHTM MSc in Global Mental Health, which opens a new field but requires a different kind of fluency and a different network?

These are not equally good paths to the same destination. They are different destinations. The question is which one I'm actually choosing.

06 · Portfolio integrity
If I move toward global health, what happens to the portfolio? Does Make Space still make sense? Does Dear Ordinary still have a home? Or does the reorientation require me to let some things go — and am I willing to do that?

A portfolio career survives reorientation only if the switching between modes still generates genuine insight. The question is whether the new direction is additive or substitutive.

Phase Two · Years 5 to 12 · Becoming a Contributor

07 · Research identity
By the time I have a body of graduate work behind me, have I produced anything — even a conceptual paper, even a short commentary — that the global mental health research community has engaged with? If not, why not?

Peer-reviewed publication is the currency of research credibility. The question is not whether I want to publish, but whether I'm producing work that the field finds useful enough to engage with.

08 · Creative health as method
Is the creative health work I'm already doing — Make Space, the Other Bodies tools, the facilitation practice — something I can translate into a rigorous intervention model that could be tested and adapted in low-resource settings? Or is it practice that belongs to a different part of my life?

Arts-based approaches to depression and anxiety have a genuine evidence base in global mental health, particularly in humanitarian contexts. But translating a personal practice into a scalable, culturally adaptable intervention is a specific kind of research work.

09 · Doctoral direction
When I choose a doctoral program, am I choosing it because it produces the kind of researcher I want to be — or because it produces the credential I think I need? Do those point to the same program?

A clinical psychology PhD produces licensed practitioners with research training. A global mental health or health systems PhD produces researchers and systems influencers. These are different people doing different work.

10 · Genuine partnership
Have I built any genuine relationships with researchers or practitioners working in global mental health in low-and-middle-income countries — relationships where I am learning from them as much as contributing to them?

The field's most important critique of itself is that it has historically been shaped by researchers from wealthy countries studying populations elsewhere. The question of how to enter this world without reproducing that dynamic is not rhetorical. It requires actual relationships.

11 · Task-shifting fit
Is training non-specialist community health workers to deliver evidence-based mental health support the problem I most want to work on — or is it one version of the problem I'm using as a placeholder until something more specific becomes clear?

Task-shifting is the most tractable approach to the global mental health treatment gap. But working in it requires specific expertise in intervention design, cultural adaptation, and implementation science. Is that where my energy actually goes?

12 · Disability and global health
Does my disability justice work — the accessibility expertise, the UDL practice, the CPACC credential — have a meaningful role in global health work, or have I moved away from that as a central focus?

Disability intersects with the global mental health treatment gap in ways that are seriously underresearched. People with disabilities are disproportionately excluded from mental health systems that already exclude most people. This is a genuine gap. Is it mine to work on?

Phase Three · Years 12 to 20 · Operating at Scale

13 · Theory of change
At this stage, can I articulate clearly and honestly how my specific work reduces suffering at population scale — not in aspiration, but in evidence? Who has been helped, by how much, and how do I know?

This is the question that separates researchers who contribute from researchers who produce. The answer requires data, honesty, and enough humility to revise the work when it isn't working.

14 · The integrative centre, revisited
Has the original vision of an integrative mental health centre transformed into something with international scope and research mandate — or has it remained a Vancouver-based clinical and community space? Is either outcome a failure, or are they both valid expressions of the same underlying purpose?

Visions are allowed to evolve. The question is whether the evolution is a response to genuine learning or a retreat from ambition.

15 · The portfolio at twenty years
Am I still switching gears? Is Dear Ordinary still alive? Is there still a studio practice, a creative health facilitation, a body of art being made alongside the research and the systems work — or did the global health reorientation quietly collapse the portfolio into a single channel?

The portfolio model survives only if the switching is protected as a discipline over decades, not just years. The creative practice is not decoration. It is epistemology. The question is whether it is still being treated that way.

16 · The question underneath all of it
Looking back from here: did I choose this path because it was where I could do the most good, or because it was where I felt most fully myself — and have I made peace with the possibility that those might not have always been the same answer?

80,000 Hours asks for impact maximization. A life asks for something more whole than that. The question is not which one wins. It is how honestly I have held both.

Depression is the leading cause of disability worldwide. This is the plan for working on it — locally, in the room with a person, and at the scale where systems change. Each step includes options so the path can flex without losing its direction.

Learning enough of that language to read the evidence, collaborate with researchers, and generate credible knowledge about depression is what this path is for. Every step here serves the clinical and creative work at the centre of the career.

Orientation
What This Is — and What It Isn't

Research literacy in service of clinical purpose. Not a retraining as a population scientist. The question at every step: does this make me more effective at helping people with depression and building systems that reach more of them?

FramingRead First
The Distinction That Matters
Epidemiologist vs. clinician-researcher with epidemiological literacy — these are different people doing different work

An epidemiologist designs surveillance systems, models disease distribution, and produces the statistical evidence that tells us how many people are affected and why. Most never sit with a patient. That is not this path.

What you are building
Clinician-researcher with research fluency
A psychologist who works directly with people experiencing depression, generates evidence about what helps them, and can collaborate credibly with research teams — including those who use epidemiological methods. The clinical work is primary. The research fluency serves it.
The analogy
Reading a financial statement vs. being an accountant
A lawyer who can read a balance sheet is not an accountant. They are a lawyer with a useful additional literacy. Research methods training here is exactly this — a literacy that makes collaboration possible, not a disciplinary identity change.
The test at every step
Does this serve the person in the room?
Every research methods skill acquired should be traceable back to this question: does understanding this make me better at helping people with depression, training others to do the same, or building systems that reach people currently unreached? If the answer is unclear, the step may not be necessary.
The model
Vikram Patel — clinician, researcher, advocate
Sees patients. Runs randomised trials. Trains community practitioners. Advises WHO. Writes books for general readers. Uses epidemiological evidence to make the case for what he already knows from clinical work. This is the shape of the career, not the epidemiologist's shape.
Layer One · Now to Year 2 · Minimum Necessary
Reading the Evidence — Becoming Fluent in the Field's Language

The smallest possible investment that gives you enough to engage with the global mental health research literature, collaborate with researchers, and write work that the field takes seriously.

Step 01Now
Read the Evidence Base for Depression Treatment
Self-directed · No prerequisites · Immediate · Transforms how you read everything else

Before any formal methods training, read the research that defines the current state of depression treatment — not to critique the methods, but to understand what the evidence currently says and where the gaps are. This is the content knowledge that makes methods training meaningful.

Treatment evidence
Cochrane Reviews on Psychological Treatments for Depression
The Cochrane Collaboration produces systematic reviews of the best available evidence. The depression reviews — on CBT, behavioural activation, interpersonal therapy, and arts-based approaches — are the most rigorous summaries of what works, for whom, and at what effect size. Free online.
Task-shifting evidence
Patel et al. — Healthy Activity Program and Thinking Healthy Program trials
The core empirical evidence for task-shifted depression treatment in low-resource settings. Reading these trials shows you exactly how randomised controlled trials work in global mental health — from the inside of a real, consequential research program.
Creative health evidence
Fancourt, Finn & Warran — arts, social prescribing, and depression outcomes
Daisy Fancourt's body of work using epidemiological datasets to study the relationship between arts engagement and depression. This is your field's evidence base — understand it thoroughly before building on it.
Systems evidence
WHO Mental Health Action Plan 2013–2030
The international policy framework for mental health systems. Understanding what governments and multilateral bodies are committed to — and where implementation is failing — tells you where the leverage points are for systems-level work on depression.
Step 02Year 1
LSHTM — Epidemiology for Public Health
Online · Part-time · The foundational vocabulary · Serves everything that follows

The first and most important of the three LSHTM short courses. Gives you the conceptual vocabulary for how health outcomes are measured at population level — not so you can design studies yourself at this stage, but so you can read them, critique them, and understand what they are and are not claiming.

Course
Epidemiology for Public Health — LSHTM Online
Covers study design, measures of disease frequency, measures of association, bias, confounding, and causation. The concepts here appear in every global mental health paper you will ever read. Understanding them is the difference between engaging with evidence and deferring to it.
What it gives you
The vocabulary to read depression research critically
After this course you can read a randomised controlled trial on a depression intervention and understand the study design, the outcome measures, the limitations, and what the effect size actually means. This is the minimum threshold for credible research collaboration.
What it does not do
Make you an epidemiologist
This is one short course. It gives you literacy, not expertise. The goal is to be able to engage with epidemiological researchers as an intelligent collaborator — not to compete with them on their own methodological ground.
Alternative
Epidemiology: The Basic Science of Public Health — UNC on Coursera
Lower cost, more flexible. Less prestigious than LSHTM but covers the core concepts. A reasonable alternative if timing or cost is a barrier — do the LSHTM course when you apply to the MSc.

Purpose check: this course exists on this path because the global mental health treatment gap for depression is documented in epidemiological terms — DALYs, prevalence, incidence, treatment coverage rates. Understanding those terms lets you use them in your own writing, your grant applications, and your conversations with researchers who take them for granted.

Step 03Year 1–2
LSHTM — Introduction to Statistics for Public Health
Online · Part-time · Reading trials with confidence · Not becoming a statistician

The second LSHTM short course. The goal is specific and bounded: to be able to read the results section of a clinical trial on a depression intervention and understand what the numbers mean — effect sizes, confidence intervals, p-values, what the statistical model is actually doing.

Course
Introduction to Statistics for Public Health — LSHTM Online
Descriptive statistics, probability, hypothesis testing, regression, and how these tools are applied in health research. Not a mathematics course — a reasoning course. The emphasis is on what statistical results mean and when to trust them.
Practical application
Reading the Fancourt depression papers with new eyes
After this course, return to Daisy Fancourt's key papers. The statistical models she uses — multivariable regression on large cohort data — will now be legible to you in a way they weren't before. This is the direct payoff for the clinical and creative health work you're building toward.
What this is not
Training to conduct statistical analyses independently
Research psychologists collaborate with biostatisticians for complex analyses. Your role is to understand the outputs well enough to interpret them, question them, and communicate them — not to run the models yourself. A good research team has a statistician. You bring the clinical and intervention knowledge.
Layer Two · MSc Years · Applied Research Training
Generating Evidence — From Reading to Producing

The MSc in Global Mental Health is where research literacy becomes research competence. The thesis is not a hoop to jump through — it is the first substantial piece of evidence you generate about depression and creative health, and it is yours.

Step 04MSc Years
LSHTM MSc — Research Methods Training
Embedded in the degree · Qualitative and quantitative · Systematic review · The thesis as the real output

The LSHTM MSc in Global Mental Health includes formal research methods training as part of the degree. This is where the short course foundations become applicable skills. The methods you learn here are chosen because they are the ones used to study depression in global health contexts — not methods training in the abstract.

Qualitative methods
Thematic analysis, grounded theory, framework analysis
Qualitative methods are particularly relevant for studying how creative and expressive approaches to depression are experienced by participants — the texture of what it feels like to engage with art-making as a pathway through depression. These are methods you can conduct independently and that align with your existing practice knowledge.
Mixed methods
Combining quantitative outcomes with qualitative experience
The most powerful research in creative health uses both — outcome measures alongside in-depth accounts of experience. Mixed methods is increasingly the standard for intervention research. Understanding how to design studies that hold both is a genuine skill that few researchers have.
Systematic review
What is already known about creative approaches to depression
A systematic review of the evidence on arts-based and creative interventions for depression would be a substantial contribution to the field and a publishable thesis chapter. It maps the gap, demonstrates rigorous methods, and positions you as someone who knows the literature thoroughly.
The thesis
Your first original contribution to the evidence base
Design the thesis around creative or arts-based approaches to depression in underserved or low-resource settings. This is not a detour from the clinical work — it is the first piece of evidence that the clinical work will eventually rest on. Choose a methods approach that you can execute well and that produces something genuinely useful.

Purpose check: research methods training in the MSc exists to give you the tools to produce your own evidence about depression and creative health — not to make you fluent in methods you will never use. Choose methods that match both the questions you are asking and the skills you already have from fifteen years of applied inquiry.

Step 05MSc Years
Implementation Science — The Methods That Match Your Work
How interventions move from evidence to practice · The research home for what you are actually doing

Implementation science is the study of how evidence-based interventions are adopted, adapted, and sustained in real-world settings. It is the methodological field that most directly matches the work you are building toward — taking what works for depression and designing the systems that make it available at scale.

Why this matters
The gap between efficacy and reach is an implementation problem
We have effective treatments for depression. The global treatment gap exists not because the treatments don't work but because the systems to deliver them don't exist at the necessary scale. Implementation science is the discipline that studies why this happens and what to do about it. Your learning design expertise is directly applicable here.
Key frameworks
CFIR, RE-AIM, Consolidated Framework for Implementation Research
These are the dominant frameworks for studying how interventions are implemented. Learning to use them gives you a structured way to study how creative health approaches to depression are taken up — or not — in different settings and by different practitioners.
Entry point
Implementation Science — BioMed Central journal
Open access. Read the papers on mental health intervention implementation specifically. This is the literature where your learning design expertise and your depression focus meet most naturally — and where your conceptual paper has a genuine home.
Online training
National Implementation Research Network — Free Resources
Free online materials on implementation science concepts and frameworks. A useful self-directed introduction before formal coursework in the MSc or doctoral program.
Layer Three · Doctoral Years · Research Independence
Producing Original Knowledge — The Dissertation and Beyond

The doctoral program is where research literacy becomes research independence. The methods training here is embedded in the supervision relationship and shaped entirely by the question the dissertation is asking about depression.

Step 06PhD Years
Clinical Research Methods — Embedded in the PhD
Randomised controlled trials · Feasibility studies · Single-case experimental design · Chosen for the question, not the prestige

Clinical psychology doctoral programs include formal research methods training. The methods you develop during the PhD should be chosen because they are the right tools for the research question — not because they are the most sophisticated methods available. The question is: what is the most credible way to study whether creative approaches to depression work, and for whom?

Feasibility and pilot studies
Testing creative interventions before scaling them
Before a full randomised trial, a well-designed feasibility study asks: can this intervention be delivered as intended? Do participants engage? What outcomes should a larger trial measure? This is the right scale for a dissertation on creative approaches to depression — rigorous, achievable, and genuinely useful to the field.
Single-case experimental design
Studying individual response to creative depression interventions
Particularly well-suited to clinical psychology research on new interventions. Allows rigorous study of whether a creative health approach is working for a specific person over time — bridges the gap between clinical observation and research evidence.
Qualitative methods — deepened
Interpretative phenomenological analysis, narrative inquiry
What is the lived experience of engaging with creative and expressive practices as a pathway through depression? These methods are the ones that can answer that question — and they draw on the same sensibility as the fine art and facilitation practice. They are not lesser methods than quantitative approaches. They answer different questions.
Collaboration model
Biostatistician as collaborator, not dependency
The doctoral program will have biostatistical support available. For quantitative components of the dissertation, collaborate with a statistician rather than attempting to become one. Your contribution is the clinical and intervention knowledge — their contribution is the analytical expertise. This is how research teams work.

Purpose check: every method developed in the PhD should be traceable to a specific question about depression that it is the right tool to answer. Methods training is not an end in itself — it is in service of generating knowledge that improves how depression is understood and treated.

Step 07PhD Years
Grant Writing as Applied Research Literacy
CIHR · SSHRC · Wellcome · The skill that funds the work and proves the thinking

Writing a research grant is the most demanding test of research literacy because it requires you to demonstrate — to expert reviewers — that you understand the evidence base, can identify a genuine gap, and have the methods to address it. It is also the practical skill that makes an independent research program possible.

Primary funder
CIHR — Canadian Institutes of Health Research
The primary federal funder of health research in Canada. CIHR funds mental health research including psychosocial and arts-based interventions. Understanding how to write for CIHR is a doctoral-phase skill worth developing early — apply for graduate fellowships and small project grants before the main research program.
Arts and health angle
Wellcome Trust — Mental Health Priority Area
The Wellcome Trust has made mental health a strategic priority and funds innovative approaches including arts-based and creative interventions. Relevant for the international dimension of the research program — particularly work in low-and-middle-income country contexts.
Humanities angle
SSHRC — Social Sciences and Humanities Research Council
The creative health and arts-based intervention work sits at the intersection of health and humanities — making SSHRC a relevant funder alongside CIHR. Understanding how to frame the work for both agencies increases the funding surface area substantially.
Layer Four · Throughout · What Research Literacy Actually Produces
The Outputs That Make the Work Travel

Research literacy is not an internal achievement — it is what allows the clinical and creative work to generate knowledge that others can build on. These are the forms that knowledge takes.

Step 08Ongoing
Publication — Starting Now, Growing Over Time
Conceptual paper first · Thesis chapter second · Empirical studies during and after the PhD

The publication record builds gradually and each piece reflects the stage of training. The conceptual paper requires no university affiliation and can be written now. The systematic review emerges from the MSc. The empirical studies come from the PhD. The progression is the story of growing research independence in service of a single coherent question about depression.

Now
Conceptual paper — learning systems and accessible depression care
No university affiliation required. The argument that only you can make: that designing the training systems which deliver depression interventions is as important as designing the interventions themselves — and that accessibility must be built into both. Target Global Mental Health or Arts in Psychotherapy.
MSc years
Systematic review or scoping review — creative approaches to depression
What does the evidence currently show about arts-based and creative interventions for depression? A rigorous systematic review answers this question and becomes the foundation for the doctoral research. Publishable as a stand-alone paper.
PhD years
Empirical studies from the dissertation
The dissertation generates at least two to three publishable papers — typically a theoretical framing paper, an empirical study, and a discussion of implications. Each chapter of a well-designed dissertation is a paper in preparation. This is how the publication record grows substantively.
Throughout
Accessible Learning Labs Substack as public scholarship
The Substack is not separate from the research record — it is its public face. Writing clearly about depression, creative health, and accessible care for a practitioner audience is a form of knowledge translation that academic publications cannot do. Both matter. Both compound.
Step 09Ongoing
Conference Presence — Entering the Conversation
Global mental health · Arts and health · Implementation science · The network built in person

Research literacy includes knowing where the conversations in your field happen and being present in them. Conference presentations and posters are how early-career researchers become known to the people whose collaboration and mentorship they need. Bursaries are available — applying for them is itself a form of research writing practice.

Primary
Global Mental Health — Movement for Global Mental Health events
The community most directly aligned with the depression focus and the task-shifting approach. Attending and eventually presenting here builds the international network that the research program depends on.
Creative health
World Alliance for Arts Education — Arts and Health track
The international community for arts in health contexts. Presenting Make Space and the Other Bodies tools here places the creative health work in an academic and professional context — and connects you to researchers and practitioners working in the same space globally.
Canadian anchor
CAMH Research Symposia and Canadian mental health conferences
Building the domestic research network alongside the international one. CAMH hosts regular research symposia. The Canadian Association for Mental Health and Canadian Psychological Association conferences are the domestic forums where the clinical and research work become visible to Canadian collaborators and funders.
The Through-Line
Every Method in Service of One Question

Depression is the leading cause of disability worldwide. More than three quarters of people experiencing it in low-income countries receive no care at all. The research literacy built across this path exists to generate knowledge that closes that gap — locally, in the room, and at the scale where it matters.

AnchorAlways
The Test at Every Decision Point
Does this make me more effective at helping people with depression — in the room and at scale?

Research methods training is a means, not an end. At every decision point — which course to take, which methods to learn, which conferences to attend, which papers to write — the orienting question is the same.

Clinical
Does this make me better at sitting with a depressed person?
The clinical work is primary. Research training that deepens the clinical understanding — by revealing what the evidence shows about depression, what works, and for whom — serves this directly. Research training that pulls attention away from the clinical relationship without obvious payoff should be questioned.
Systems
Does this help me design systems that reach people who currently can't access care?
Implementation science, learning systems design, and task-shifting research all serve this question directly. Methods that illuminate how to make depression treatment more accessible — across resource levels, cultures, and delivery contexts — are load-bearing on this path.
Creative
Does this deepen my understanding of why creative approaches to depression work?
The creative health practice needs a research foundation to travel beyond the room where it happens. Methods that build that foundation — qualitative studies of participant experience, feasibility trials, systematic reviews of the arts and depression evidence base — are directly in service of the core purpose.
Portfolio
Does this keep the switching intact?
Research literacy should not collapse the portfolio into a single analytical mode. The fine art practice, the facilitation, the creative health work — these generate a kind of knowledge about depression that methods training cannot. Both are necessary. The test is whether the research work is enriching the creative work or quietly replacing it.

You are not training to become an epidemiologist. You are training to become a psychologist, researcher, and practitioner who understands depression deeply enough — from the inside of clinical work, from the inside of creative practice, and from the inside of the research evidence — to contribute something that the field genuinely needs and currently lacks.
