What This Is — and What It Is Not

The distinction that shapes every decision in this tab. Epidemiology is a discipline. Research literacy in service of clinical purpose is a skill set. These are not the same thing.

FramingStart Here
The Clinician-Researcher — Not the Epidemiologist
Vikram Patel as the model · Research fluency in service of the clinical and creative work

An epidemiologist designs surveillance systems, models disease distribution, and produces population-level statistical evidence. Most never sit with a patient. That is not this path.

What is being built
Clinician-researcher with research fluency
A psychologist who works directly with people experiencing depression, generates evidence about what helps them, and can collaborate credibly with research teams. The clinical work is primary. The research fluency serves it.
The analogy
A lawyer who can read a financial statement

Vikram Patel

Every research methods skill acquired should be traceable back to this: does understanding this make me better at helping people with depression, training others to do the same, or building systems that reach the people currently unreached?
Layer One · Now to Year 2
Reading the Evidence — Fluency in the Field's Language

The smallest possible investment that gives enough to engage with the global mental health research literature, collaborate with researchers, and write work the field takes seriously.

Step 01Now
Read the Depression Evidence Base
Self-directed · No prerequisites · Makes methods training meaningful

Before any formal methods training, read the research that defines the current state of depression treatment — to understand what the evidence says and where the gaps are. This is the content knowledge that makes methods training legible.

Treatment evidence
Cochrane Reviews — Psychological Treatments for Depression
Systematic reviews of CBT, behavioural activation, interpersonal therapy, and arts-based approaches. The most rigorous summaries of what works, for whom, and at what effect size. Free online. Read the depression reviews first.
Task-shifting evidence
Patel et al. — Healthy Activity Program and Thinking Healthy Program
The core empirical evidence for task-shifted depression treatment in low-resource settings. Reading these trials shows exactly how randomised controlled trials work in global mental health — from the inside of a real, consequential research program.
Creative health evidence
Daisy Fancourt — full publication record on arts and depression
Using large epidemiological datasets to study the relationship between arts engagement and depression outcomes. This is the evidence base the creative health work will eventually contribute to. Understand it thoroughly before building on it.
Policy
WHO Mental Health Action Plan 2013–2030
What governments and multilateral bodies are committed to — and where implementation is failing. Understanding this tells you where the leverage points are for systems-level work on depression.
Step 02Year 1
LSHTM — Epidemiology for Public Health
Online · The conceptual vocabulary · Not to design studies — to understand them

The foundational course. Study design, measures of disease frequency, measures of association, bias, confounding, causation. These concepts appear in every global mental health paper on depression. Understanding them is the difference between engaging with evidence and deferring to it.

Primary
Epidemiology for Public Health — LSHTM Online
Covers study design, measures of disease frequency and association, bias, confounding, and causation. The vocabulary for reading and critiquing depression research. The minimum foundation for the statistics course that follows.
Alternative
Epidemiology: The Basic Science of Public Health — UNC on Coursera
Lower cost, more flexible. Less prestigious than LSHTM but covers the core concepts. A reasonable alternative if timing or cost creates a barrier — do the LSHTM course when applying to the MSc.
Purpose check
Why this course is on this path
The global mental health treatment gap for depression is documented in epidemiological terms — DALYs, prevalence, incidence, treatment coverage rates. Understanding those terms lets you use them in your own writing, grant applications, and conversations with researchers who take them for granted.

This course exists not to produce an epidemiologist but to produce someone who can walk into a global mental health research meeting and understand what is being discussed.

Step 03Year 1–2
LSHTM — Introduction to Statistics for Public Health
Online · Reading trials with confidence · Not becoming a statistician

The goal is specific and bounded: to be able to read the results section of a clinical trial on a depression intervention and understand what the numbers mean — effect sizes, confidence intervals, p-values, what the statistical model is actually doing.

Course
Introduction to Statistics for Public Health — LSHTM Online
Descriptive statistics, probability, hypothesis testing, regression — applied in health research. Not a mathematics course. A reasoning course. The emphasis is on what statistical results mean and when to trust them.
Practical payoff
Reading the Fancourt depression papers with new eyes
After this course, return to Daisy Fancourt's key papers. The multivariable regression models she uses on large cohort data will now be legible in a way they weren't before. This is the direct payoff for the clinical and creative health work being built toward.
What this is not
Training to conduct statistical analyses independently
Research psychologists collaborate with biostatisticians for complex analyses. The role is to understand the outputs well enough to interpret them, question them, and communicate them. A good research team has a statistician. The clinical and intervention knowledge is the contribution here.

Step 04MSc Years
Research Methods Training — Embedded in the MSc
Qualitative and quantitative · Implementation science · The thesis as the real output

The LSHTM MSc includes formal research methods training. The methods learned here are chosen because they are the ones used to study depression in global health contexts — not methods training in the abstract.

Qualitative methods
Thematic analysis · Grounded theory · Framework analysis
Particularly relevant for studying how creative and expressive approaches to depression are experienced by participants — the texture of what it feels like to engage with art-making as a pathway through depression. Methods that can be conducted independently and that align with existing practice knowledge.
Mixed methods
Outcome measures alongside in-depth accounts of experience
The most powerful research in creative health uses both. Mixed methods is increasingly the standard for intervention research. Understanding how to design studies that hold quantitative and qualitative evidence together is a genuine skill that few researchers have.
Implementation science
How interventions move from evidence into practice
The study of why evidence-based depression interventions fail to reach most people who need them — and what to do about it. The methodological field that most directly matches the systems-level work being built toward. Learning design expertise is directly applicable here.
The thesis
First original contribution to the evidence base
Design it around creative or arts-based approaches to depression in underserved or low-resource settings. The methods approach should be one that can be executed well and produces something genuinely useful — not the most sophisticated method available but the right one for the question being asked.
Layer Three · Doctoral Years
Research Independence — The Dissertation and the Grant

Doctoral methods training is shaped entirely by the dissertation question. Every method developed should be traceable to something specific that question requires.

Step 05PhD Years
Clinical Research Methods — Chosen for the Question
Feasibility studies · Single-case experimental design · Qualitative deepened · Biostatistician as collaborator

The methods developed in the PhD should be the right tools for the research question — not the most sophisticated methods available. The question is: what is the most credible way to study whether creative approaches to depression work, and for whom?

Feasibility studies
Testing creative interventions before scaling them
Before a full randomised trial, a feasibility study asks: can this intervention be delivered as intended? Do participants engage? What outcomes should a larger trial measure? This is the right scale for a dissertation on creative approaches to depression — rigorous, achievable, and genuinely useful to the field.
Single-case design
Studying individual response to creative depression interventions
Well-suited to clinical psychology research on new interventions. Allows rigorous study of whether a creative health approach is working for a specific person over time. Bridges the gap between clinical observation and research evidence.
Qualitative — deepened
Interpretative phenomenological analysis · Narrative inquiry
What is the lived experience of engaging with creative and expressive practices as a pathway through depression? These methods answer that question — and draw on the same sensibility as the fine art and facilitation practice. Not lesser methods. Different questions.
Collaboration model
Biostatistician as collaborator, not dependency
The doctoral program has biostatistical support. For quantitative components, collaborate with a statistician rather than attempting to become one. The clinical and intervention knowledge is the contribution. Their expertise is the analytical rigour. This is how research teams work.
Step 06PhD Years
Grant Writing as Applied Research Literacy
CIHR · SSHRC · Wellcome · The skill that funds the work and proves the thinking

Writing a research grant is the most demanding test of research literacy — it requires demonstrating to expert reviewers that you understand the evidence base, can identify a genuine gap, and have the methods to address it. It is also the practical skill that makes an independent research program possible.

Primary funder
CIHR — Canadian Institutes of Health Research
The primary federal funder of health research in Canada. Funds mental health research including psychosocial and arts-based interventions. Apply for graduate fellowships and small project grants before the main research program. Practice the genre early.
Arts and health
Wellcome Trust — Mental Health Priority Area
Has made mental health a strategic priority and funds innovative approaches including arts-based interventions. Relevant for the international dimension of the research — particularly work in low-and-middle-income country contexts.
Humanities angle
SSHRC — Social Sciences and Humanities Research Council
The creative health and arts-based intervention work sits at the intersection of health and humanities — making SSHRC a relevant funder alongside CIHR. Framing work for both agencies increases the funding surface area substantially.
Layer Four · Throughout
The Outputs — What Research Literacy Actually Produces

Research literacy is not an internal achievement. It is what allows the clinical and creative work to generate knowledge that others can build on.

Step 07Ongoing
Publication — Starting Now, Growing Over Time
Conceptual paper first · Thesis second · Empirical studies during and after the PhD

The publication record builds gradually and each piece reflects the stage of training. The progression is the story of growing research independence in service of one coherent question about depression.

Now
Conceptual paper — learning systems and accessible depression care
No university affiliation required. Write it now. The argument the field has not made: that designing the training systems delivering depression interventions is as important as designing the interventions themselves, and accessibility must be built into both.
MSc years
Systematic review — creative approaches to depression
What does the evidence show about arts-based and creative interventions for depression? A rigorous systematic review answers this question, becomes the thesis, and is published as a stand-alone paper — the evidence map the dissertation builds on.
PhD years
Empirical studies from the dissertation
The dissertation generates two to three publishable papers. Each chapter is a paper in preparation. This is how the publication record grows substantively — not through isolated efforts but through a single sustained research program.
Throughout
Accessible Learning Labs Substack as public scholarship
Not separate from the research record — its public face. Writing clearly about depression, creative health, and accessible care for a practitioner audience is knowledge translation that academic publications cannot do. Both matter. Both compound.
AnchorAlways
The Test at Every Decision Point
Does this make me more effective at helping people with depression — in the room and at scale?

Research methods training is a means, not an end. At every decision point — which course to take, which methods to develop, which papers to write — the orienting question is the same.

Clinical
Does this make me better at sitting with a depressed person?
Research training that deepens the clinical understanding — by revealing what the evidence shows about depression, what works, and for whom — serves this directly. Research training that pulls attention away from the clinical relationship without obvious payoff should be questioned.
Systems
Does this help design systems that reach people who currently can't access care?
Implementation science, learning systems design, and task-shifting research all serve this question directly. Methods that illuminate how to make depression treatment more accessible — across resource levels, cultures, and delivery contexts — are load-bearing on this path.
Creative
Does this deepen understanding of why creative approaches to depression work?
The creative health practice needs a research foundation to travel beyond the room where it happens. Methods that build that foundation — qualitative studies of participant experience, feasibility trials, systematic reviews — are in direct service of the core purpose.
Portfolio
Does this keep the switching intact?
Research literacy should not collapse the portfolio into a single analytical mode. The fine art practice, the facilitation, the creative health work generate a kind of knowledge about depression that methods training cannot. Both are necessary. The test is whether research work is enriching the creative work or quietly replacing it.

This is not a path toward becoming an epidemiologist. It is a path toward becoming a psychologist, researcher, and practitioner who understands depression deeply enough — from the inside of clinical work, from the inside of creative practice, and from the inside of the research evidence — to contribute something the field genuinely needs and currently lacks.

Every serious career plan has genuine weaknesses. Naming them honestly — and working with them rather than around them — is what separates a plan that holds over twenty years from one that quietly collapses under its own ambition. These are the real challenges, assessed through the 80,000 Hours lens, with options for turning each one into deeper competence.

The challenges below are not reasons to abandon the path. They are the friction points where the most important decisions will be made. A thoughtful, senior person in the global mental health field — someone who cares about the problem and has no stake in making you feel good — would raise every one of these. They are raised here first, so that the answers can be built into the path rather than discovered too late.

Challenge 01Timeline
The credential runway is long and the deployment is late
12+ years to full clinical and research authority · Opportunity cost accumulates · The field moves while you train

80,000 Hours is consistently cautious about long credential sequences. Every year spent building is a year not deploying. A twelve-year runway to full authority means the most high-leverage work — training practitioners at scale, influencing policy, leading a research program — does not begin until the mid-career point at earliest. The depression treatment gap does not wait. The field evolves. The specific gaps you are training to fill may look different in twelve years than they do today.

The specific risks
Risk A
Credential obsolescence
The global mental health field's understanding of what works for depression will continue to evolve. The dissertation question you identify now may be partially answered by others before you complete it. The specific gap you're training to fill may shift.
Risk B
Opportunity cost of sequencing
Leeds MSc, then LSHTM short courses, then LSHTM MSc, then MA, then PhD is a waterfall structure. Delays at any stage compound. A part-time MA that takes four years instead of two delays the PhD by two years and pushes clinical licensure into your mid-fifties.
Risk C
The field moves on
Task-shifting, creative health, and implementation science are growing areas now. In twelve years, the specific intersection you're building toward may be crowded rather than neglected. The advantage of being early is only captured if you deploy early enough to establish it.
Risk D
Burnout before arrival
Twelve years of part-time study alongside a career, a consulting practice, and a portfolio of creative projects is an enormous sustained demand. The risk is not failure to complete — it is arriving at the PhD exhausted rather than energised, with the most important work still ahead.
Options — turning this challenge into positioning
Option A
Deploy at every stage, not just at the end
The conceptual paper now. The Other Bodies tools paper during Leeds. The systematic review as the LSHTM thesis. Each credential produces a publication. By the time the PhD is complete, there is already a body of work — not an empty runway followed by a launch. The field knows you before you arrive with the credential.
Option B
Build in explicit 2-year decision points
At the end of Leeds, assess whether the LSHTM MSc is still the right next step or whether the MA has become more urgent. At the end of the LSHTM MSc, assess whether the PhD should precede or follow the MA. The plan is the starting hypothesis. The decision points are where it gets updated with real information.
Option C
Consider whether Leeds and LSHTM can overlap
Leeds is part-time online over three years. The LSHTM short courses are also part-time online. It may be possible to begin the LSHTM short courses in year two of Leeds rather than waiting until Leeds is complete — compressing the timeline by one to two years without increasing peak load.
Option D
Treat the learning design career as leverage, not just income
A senior learning design role in the health sector, applied to mental health programming, is not waiting to become relevant — it is already the work. Framing the day job as active deployment, not a holding pattern, changes the psychological experience of the credential runway and produces real systems-level impact throughout.

The timeline challenge is real but manageable. The key discipline is treating every stage as deployment, not preparation. A person who arrives at the PhD with three published papers, an international network, a running facilitation program, and a health sector career behind them is not behind. They are unusually well-positioned.

Challenge 02Evidence
The arts-based depression evidence base is thinner than the enthusiasm suggests
RCT evidence is limited · Effect sizes are modest · The field is promising, not settled

The randomised controlled trial evidence for arts-based interventions specifically for depression is weaker than the enthusiasm in creative health circles suggests. Most of the strong evidence is for CBT, behavioural activation, and interpersonal therapy. Fancourt's epidemiological work is rigorous and important, but it establishes association, not mechanism. A peer reviewer at a top journal, or a grant panel at CIHR, will know this. Building a research career on a thin evidence base is a real risk — the work may amount to confirming that the evidence is limited rather than advancing it.

The specific risks
Risk A
Confirmation bias in the research agenda
Someone who has invested fifteen years in creative practice and facilitation has a strong prior belief that creative approaches work. That prior belief can shape research design, interpretation, and publication choices in ways that are invisible from the inside but obvious to external reviewers.
Risk B
Null results are possible and important
The systematic review thesis may find that the evidence for arts-based approaches to depression is weaker than expected. The feasibility study in the PhD may find that creative interventions don't outperform active control conditions. These are genuine scientific possibilities, not catastrophes — but they require a research agenda that can hold them.
Risk C
The field may consolidate before you arrive
Several well-funded research groups in the UK and US are actively working on arts and health, including depression. By the time the PhD is complete, the systematic review may already exist, the feasibility trials may already be done, and the implementation science questions may be further advanced than anticipated.
Options — turning this challenge into positioning
Option A
Make the thin evidence base the research question, not the assumption
The most credible position is not "arts-based approaches work for depression and I am building the systems to scale them" — it is "the evidence is promising and contested, and my research program will clarify what we actually know and for whom." That is a stronger scientific position and a more honest one.
Option B
Study mechanisms, not just outcomes
The most important gap in creative health research is not whether outcomes improve — it is why, for whom, and under what conditions. Mechanism-focused research (what does creative engagement do to the neural, psychological, and social processes involved in depression?) is less crowded, more fundable, and directly leverages the neurobiology background.
Option C
Anchor in implementation science rather than efficacy
The implementation question — how do you design training systems that allow community practitioners to deliver creative health approaches at scale — does not require the efficacy question to be fully settled. It is a tractable research question regardless of whether the intervention effect sizes are large or modest, and it is where the learning design expertise is most directly applicable.
Option D
Design the systematic review to be genuinely neutral
The MSc thesis systematic review should be designed to find whatever the evidence shows — including evidence that creative approaches don't work as well as hoped. Pre-registering the protocol on PROSPERO before beginning is a public commitment to that neutrality, and a signal to reviewers that the research is rigorous rather than advocacy dressed as science.

The evidence base challenge is actually the strongest argument for this research career. If the evidence were settled, the gap would be filled and the contribution would be marginal. The thinness of the evidence is the opportunity — provided the research is designed to advance it rather than simply confirm prior beliefs.

Challenge 03Field entry
Global mental health is a field with its own politics, gatekeeping, and power structures
North-South dynamics · Insider networks · The risk of arriving as an outsider with solutions

The global mental health field is not a neutral meritocracy. It has histories of researchers from wealthy countries extracting data from low-income populations without meaningful reciprocity. It has academic networks that are difficult to enter from the outside. It has debates about whose knowledge counts — and those debates are live and consequential. Arriving from Vancouver with a plan to "design scalable systems" for depression in low-income countries, without deep relationships in those contexts, risks reproducing exactly the dynamic the field is trying to move beyond.

The specific risks
Risk A
Extractive research relationships
Research partnerships with institutions in low-and-middle-income countries that are designed primarily to serve a Canadian doctoral dissertation, rather than the genuine priorities of local communities and researchers, reproduce a colonial dynamic even when intentions are good. The field is increasingly alert to this and will push back.
Risk B
Network inaccessibility
The global mental health field runs on relationships built over years at specific conferences, through specific institutions, and in specific research collaborations. The LSHTM network is the best available entry point from Canada — but it is still an entry point to a world with established insiders, and newcomers are not automatically welcomed into the most important conversations.
Risk C
The "solutions from outside" problem
A learning designer from Vancouver arriving with a framework for how community practitioners should be trained to deliver depression interventions may be received sceptically by researchers who have spent careers learning that what works in one context fails in another, and that community ownership of interventions is not optional.
Options — turning this challenge into positioning
Option A
Lead with listening, not frameworks
The first international research relationships should be built through genuine curiosity about what researchers and communities in low-resource settings say they need — not through arriving with a system to offer. The learning design expertise becomes a contribution when it is in service of locally identified priorities, not when it is imported as the solution.
Option B
Anchor the local work first
The strongest global health researchers maintain a genuine local practice — direct clinical work, community relationships, local implementation. Anchoring the research in the BC context first — with Indigenous communities, with low-income urban populations, with recent immigrants — builds the humility and local credibility that makes international work more ethical and more effective.
Option C
Build South-South and South-North relationships, not only North-South
The most interesting global mental health research is increasingly happening through South-South and South-North collaborations — researchers in India, Kenya, Brazil, and Colombia leading the work, with Canadian and UK partners in supporting roles. Positioning as a learning partner rather than an expert contributor from the outset makes the collaboration more equitable and more durable.
Option D
Engage directly with the decolonisation literature
The Leeds MSc in Disability Studies provides exactly the critical theory foundation for thinking rigorously about whose knowledge counts, who benefits from research, and what ethical collaboration actually requires. Apply that framework explicitly to the global mental health work. Show in publications and applications that this isn't naive — it is a considered position.

The field entry challenge is the most important one ethically. Getting it right matters not just for career success but for whether the work actually serves the people it claims to serve. The Leeds framework, the local clinical anchor, and the willingness to follow rather than lead in international partnerships are the response.

Challenge 04Portfolio
The portfolio model is epistemologically justified but practically fragile
Switching costs are real · Fragmentation risk · The meaning layer can become the escape hatch

The portfolio career model — learning design career, consulting practice, creative health facilitation, fine art practice, research program, clinical training — is intellectually coherent and practically ambitious. 80,000 Hours would note that each stream requires real time, real energy, and real attention. The switching between modes that is described as epistemological generates real cognitive overhead. And the streams that feel most meaningful are often the ones most likely to consume the time that the highest-leverage work requires.

The specific risks
Risk A
Dear Ordinary gets quietly sacrificed
The fine art practice is the most vulnerable stream. It has no external accountability, no deadline, no professional consequence for stopping. When Leeds coursework, the LSHTM short courses, the Substack, Make Space, and a full-time job are all running simultaneously, Dear Ordinary is the thing that gets postponed — and postponed again — until it has quietly stopped.
Risk B
The consulting practice competes with the research
Consulting income is immediate and tangible. Research outputs are slow and uncertain. During the MSc and doctoral years, when income may be constrained and research progress feels invisible, the consulting practice may expand to fill the available space — producing income but consuming the research hours that the credential sequence requires.
Risk C
Make Space becomes an avoidance mechanism
Creative facilitation is enjoyable, immediately rewarding, and produces visible impact. Doctoral research is slow, frustrating, and its impact is invisible for years. During difficult PhD years, Make Space can expand from a protected portfolio stream into the place where the hours that should be going to the dissertation actually go.
Risk D
The switching generates overhead that isn't accounted for
Moving between the analytical register of research writing and the embodied register of art-making or facilitation takes real time and energy to transition. The portfolio model assumes clean switching. In practice, the transitions consume the space that was supposed to exist between streams.
Options — turning this challenge into positioning
Option A
Protect Dear Ordinary with external accountability
The quarterly sharing structure is good but insufficient. Add a specific commitment — a date, a collaborator, a venue — that makes Dear Ordinary impossible to quietly postpone. The fine art practice is the epistemological foundation of the whole portfolio. It deserves the same structural protection as the Substack deadline or the Leeds assignment due date.
Option B
Set explicit time budgets for each stream and defend them
Not aspiration time — actual protected hours in the week, defended from the streams that expand naturally (consulting, facilitation) at the expense of the streams that don't (research writing, art practice). The budget is the discipline. Review it quarterly and name honestly which stream is being subsidised by which.
Option C
Treat each stream as having a minimum viable rhythm, not a maximum
Make Space at its minimum viable rhythm is one session per week and a monthly post. Dear Ordinary at its minimum viable rhythm is one piece per quarter. The Substack at its minimum viable rhythm is one post per week. Define the minimum for each stream and protect that minimum — rather than letting any stream expand beyond what the others can afford.
Option D
Name the hierarchy explicitly
During the Leeds and LSHTM MSc years, the hierarchy is: job, coursework, conceptual paper, Substack, Make Space, Dear Ordinary. During the MA years, clinical training comes first. During the PhD years, the dissertation comes first. Having a named hierarchy means that when streams compete for the same hours, the decision has already been made.

The portfolio is the strength of the career. But portfolios require more structural discipline than single-track careers, not less. The switching is the mechanism — but switching requires boundaries, and boundaries require named priorities. The portfolio survives only if its structure is protected, not assumed.

Challenge 05Leverage
The theory of change is not yet precise enough to evaluate
How does this career reduce depression burden at scale · The mechanism needs naming · Impact is hard to trace

80,000 Hours asks for a clear theory of change: how, specifically, does this career reduce the suffering of people who currently receive no treatment for depression? The general shape of the answer is present — clinical work, research, training systems, implementation. But the specific mechanism is not yet articulated precisely enough to evaluate. "I will train practitioners who will reach more people" is a theory of change. But how many more people? Through which training system? In which contexts? With what fidelity to the original approach? Without that precision, impact is assumed rather than designed.

The specific risks
Risk A
Busyness mistaken for impact
A portfolio career with many active streams can feel highly impactful without the impact being traceable to specific outcomes. Publishing papers, training facilitators, running sessions, consulting for health organisations — all of this can be genuinely busy without meaningfully reducing the depression burden for people who currently receive nothing.
Risk B
The highest-leverage intervention is unclear
Is the most important contribution the clinical practice? The research? The training systems? The policy influence? The answer almost certainly differs by career stage — but without naming it explicitly, the tendency is to do what is most immediately rewarding rather than what has the highest expected impact. The consulting practice is lucrative. The facilitation is immediately meaningful. The research is slow and uncertain. These incentive structures need to be named.
Risk C
The multiplier may not multiply as expected
Training one cohort of facilitators is the proof of concept for the multiplier model. But whether trained facilitators actually deliver the Make Space model with fidelity, reach people experiencing depression, and produce meaningful outcomes — rather than delivering a diluted version to people who were already moderately okay — is an empirical question that has not yet been tested.
Options — turning this challenge into positioning
Option A
Write the theory of change explicitly and update it annually
Not a vision statement — a specific causal chain. "I will develop evidence-based creative health interventions for depression, train practitioners to deliver them in low-resource settings, and publish the training systems so that other researchers can adapt and scale them, with the result that X more people per year have access to an evidence-based approach they currently cannot reach." Then test each link in that chain as the career progresses.
Option B
Identify the counterfactual contribution at each stage
At each career stage, ask: if I weren't doing this, would someone else do it just as well? For the conceptual paper — possibly. For the systematic review — probably, eventually. For the specific intersection of learning systems design, disability justice, and creative health approaches to depression — almost certainly not. Focus the most energy on the contributions that are most counterfactually significant.
Option C
Build in measurement from the beginning
Make Space with a simple outcome measure — a validated depression screening tool administered at the beginning and end of the curriculum — gives you real data about whether the program does what it claims to do. That data is the foundation of the evidence base, the grant application, the social prescribing pitch, and the honest answer to the question of whether the theory of change is working.
Option D
Apply for 80,000 Hours advising to pressure-test the theory
Their advisors are specifically trained to help people clarify their theory of change and identify the weakest links. A single advising conversation, with the theory of change written out in advance, is more useful for this purpose than months of self-assessment. Apply this week.

The theory of change challenge is not a reason to doubt the path — it is a reason to make the path more precise. The career that can articulate how it reduces suffering, at what scale, through which mechanisms, and with what evidence, is the career that attracts the best collaborators, the best funding, and the best opportunities. Precision is power here.

Challenge 06Credibility
The path requires entering multiple fields as a late-comer without established credentials
Global mental health · Clinical psychology · Academic research · Each field has its own gatekeeping

Clinical psychology doctoral programs in Canada are highly competitive and typically admit students who have completed undergraduate and master's degrees in psychology, with research experience in psychological science. The LSHTM MSc is a strong credential but it is not a psychology credential. The Leeds MSc is a strong credential but it is not a clinical credential. Arriving at a clinical psychology PhD application with a background in learning design and two MScs in disability studies and global mental health may be received as an unusual profile — which can mean either "compelling" or "does not fit our program," depending on the supervisor and the program.

The specific risks
Risk A
PhD application rejection due to non-traditional profile
Canadian clinical psychology PhD programs are accredited and regulated. They typically expect applicants to have undergraduate training in psychology, research experience in empirical psychological science, and references from psychologists. An applicant with two MScs in adjacent fields and a publication record in global mental health journals may not fit the expected profile for some programs.
Risk B
Supervisor misalignment
The doctoral program is the supervisor. A supervisor in clinical psychology whose primary research is in CBT protocols, cognitive neuroscience, or personality psychology will not be able to support a dissertation on creative approaches to depression in global health contexts — regardless of how strong the application is. Finding the right supervisor requires research, relationships, and sometimes luck.
Risk C
Credibility gaps in peer review
Publishing in clinical psychology journals requires clinical psychology credibility. Publishing in global mental health journals requires public health credibility. The intersection you're working in — arts-based approaches, implementation science, learning systems — crosses multiple disciplines, and reviewers from any one of them may find the work insufficiently rigorous in their own discipline's terms.
Options — turning this challenge into positioning
Option A
Identify the supervisor before identifying the program
The doctoral application should begin with relationship-building, not program research. Identify three to five faculty members whose research sits closest to the dissertation question — creative approaches to depression, implementation science, community-based mental health — and reach out before applying. A supervisor who wants you is more important than a program with a good ranking.
Option B
Consider a health psychology or psychological science PhD as an alternative
A PhD in health psychology, psychological science, or interdisciplinary health research may be more accessible for a non-traditional applicant and may be more appropriate for the specific research question. Clinical licensure can sometimes be pursued separately through a post-doctoral supervised practice pathway in some Canadian provinces. Check current BC College of Psychologists registration requirements.
Option C
Build the publication record before applying
The strongest non-traditional PhD application has published work. Two peer-reviewed papers — the conceptual paper and the LSHTM thesis as a systematic review — published before the application materially changes how a non-standard profile is received. They demonstrate that the research agenda is real and that the applicant can execute it.
Option D
Target programs explicitly open to interdisciplinary applicants
UBC's interdisciplinary graduate programs, SFU's Faculty of Health Sciences, and Toronto's Dalla Lana School of Public Health all have doctoral programs that are explicitly designed for researchers working across disciplinary boundaries. These may be better fits for the specific research agenda than traditional clinical psychology programs — and may lead to the same clinical registration pathway through supervised practice.

The credibility challenge is most acute at the PhD application stage. The solution is to build the publication record and the supervisory relationship before applying — so that the application is a description of a research program already underway, not a proposal for one that hasn't started.

Challenge 07Identity
The path may be serving personal meaning as much as social good — and those are not always the same
The honest question underneath all of it · Worth sitting with directly

80,000 Hours would ask this directly: is this career path designed to maximise impact on the depression treatment gap, or is it designed to maximise personal meaning and fulfilment while also being genuinely useful? These are not the same question, and the answer to which one is primary shapes every resource allocation decision. A path purely optimised for impact would look different — fewer credentials, faster deployment, more focus on training and systems and less on clinical practice, stronger orientation toward low-income country contexts from the beginning. The path as designed is richer and more whole than that — but it is worth being honest about why.

The honest questions
Question A
Is clinical licensure for impact or for authority?
The MA in Counselling Psychology and PhD in Clinical Psychology lead to Registered Psychologist status. That credential enables independent practice, supervision, and assessment. It also requires six to ten years of training. If the primary lever for reducing depression burden is training systems and research — not direct clinical practice — it is worth asking whether clinical licensure is the most efficient path to that contribution, or whether it is partly desired for the authority and identity it confers.
Question B
Is the fine art practice essential or protected?
Dear Ordinary is described as epistemologically necessary — the embodied knowledge that feeds the clinical and research work. That argument is genuine. But it is also worth asking honestly: would the research and clinical work be meaningfully worse without the fine art practice? Or is the fine art practice something that deserves to exist for its own sake, without needing to justify itself through the career? Both answers are legitimate. They just generate different planning decisions.
Question C
Is the portfolio model the most impactful structure or the most sustainable one?
A single-track career in global mental health systems — focused entirely on training infrastructure, implementation science, and policy influence — might have higher expected impact by 80,000 Hours metrics than the portfolio model. The portfolio model may be the right choice anyway — because it is sustainable, because it produces better knowledge, because it is honest about who you are. But the choice should be made consciously, not assumed.
The response that holds
Position A
Acknowledge the dual purpose honestly
This career is designed to do genuine good in the world AND to be a life worth living. 80,000 Hours ultimately accepts this — they note that personal fit, sustainability, and genuine engagement produce more impact over a career than a poorly-fitted person on a "higher-priority" path. The dual purpose is not a failure of rigor. It is honest.
Position B
Make the meaning/impact trade-offs explicit at each decision point
When the MA adds three years and the PhD adds seven, and the question is whether those ten years of clinical training are primarily for the research credential or the clinical authority or the personal formation — name that honestly. When a consulting contract is accepted at the cost of research hours, name that honestly. The discipline is not eliminating meaning from the calculus. It is making the trade-offs visible rather than invisible.
Position C
Trust that full presence produces better work
The strongest argument for the portfolio model, the fine art practice, and the clinical formation is empirical: people who are fully present in their work — who bring their whole self rather than a version optimised for impact metrics — tend to produce richer, more original, more durable contributions. The creativity, the embodied knowledge, and the personal meaning are not decorative. They are constitutive of the specific contribution only this person can make.

This challenge does not resolve. It is a question worth returning to at every two-year review, every major decision point, every time a new opportunity appears. The answer will change as the career develops and as the relative costs and benefits of each stream become clearer. The discipline is not to resolve it once but to hold it honestly throughout.

These challenges are not reasons to doubt the path. They are the friction points where the most important decisions will be made — and where the most important growth will happen. A career that cannot name its own weaknesses is a career that has not thought carefully enough. A career that names them, and builds the responses into the plan, is one that is more likely to hold over twenty years — and more likely to produce the contribution that the problem actually needs.
