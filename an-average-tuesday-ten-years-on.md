# An Average Tuesday, Ten Years On

*A day in the life of the work, written honestly — so you can feel it before it arrives.*

---

You wake before the alarm because the first call is early — it always is, when half your work happens five, eight, ten time zones ahead of a kitchen in Vancouver. You make coffee in the dark. The art on the wall is yours; the quiet is yours; the cedar light coming up grey over the inlet is the same light you've watched for years. None of that has changed. What changed is what you carry into the day.

The first verb is **listening**.

The call is with a team you've worked alongside for six years now — a district mental health program, a lead investigator who has become a friend, two facilitator-trainers who were, when you met them, simply facilitators. You don't run the call. You used to. Now you mostly listen, because the program belongs to them and the whole point of the last decade was to make yourself, in the room, unnecessary. One of the trainers, Amara, walks the team through last month's cohort — twelve new lay facilitators trained, eleven still active, the dropout a young man whose own circumstances came apart. You feel that one. You always feel the dropouts. You note it without flinching, because honest work means you don't look away from the leaks in the system you built.

After the call you do the thing that is most actually your job, the thing no job title quite names. You open a recording of a session — a facilitator in a borrowed community room, eight people in a circle, paper and cheap paint, a version of the Other Bodies prompts translated, adapted, stripped of everything that didn't travel and rebuilt around a local idiom of distress that you did not invent and could never have invented. You **watch**. This is the verb that fills more of your hours than any other and the one no one warned you about: you watch footage of rooms you will never stand in, of people you will likely never meet, being helped by someone you trained to train the person doing the helping. Three degrees of separation between you and the relief on a stranger's face. You used to think that distance would feel like loss. Mostly it feels like leverage. Occasionally, on a hard Tuesday, it still feels like loss, and you let it.

You notice something in the recording — a moment where the facilitator rushes past a participant's silence, fills it, when the silence was the work. You mark the timestamp. This is **noticing**, and it is the whole craft: the difference between a training system that produces fidelity and one that produces a thinning, diluted echo of the thing that worked in the original room. Your fifteen years of learning design were, it turns out, fifteen years of learning to see exactly this — where understanding forms and where it quietly fails to. You were building this instrument the whole time without knowing what it was for.

Mid-morning is **revising**. A module isn't landing — the section on holding a participant's disclosure without escalating into crisis, the one that has to work identically whether the facilitator has two years of practice or two weeks. The data says trainees can recite it and can't do it. So you take it apart. You rebuild it around a single video, a decision the facilitator makes in four seconds, and a structured debrief. This is unglamorous. It is most of the job. It is also the part where, if you are honest, you are happiest — the desk, the problem, the small mechanical click when a thing that wasn't transferable becomes transferable. You are, by temperament, a maker of instruments. Today the instrument is a paragraph and a clip.

Lunch is a coaching call you take walking, because you've learned the work can't all be done sitting. You're coaching a trainer who is now training her own trainers — the multiplier you sketched on a whiteboard a decade ago, made of actual people. She's good. She doesn't need you the way she did. You spend the call mostly **asking** rather than telling, which is the discipline you've had to learn most deliberately, against your own instinct to solve. The measure of your impact is no longer what you do. It's what others do without you in the room. That is a strange thing to build a sense of meaning on, and you have built it anyway.

The afternoon is the honest middle of it. You **read** fidelity data and the data is ambiguous. One site's outcomes have softened and no one is sure why — staff turnover, a funding gap, a subtle drift in how the model is being delivered, or simply the regression that follows any pilot's first bright results. You do not get to be the hero who knows. You get to sit with uncertainty and design the next question. You **write** to a co-author — a local lead whose name goes first on the paper, because the knowledge is hers as much as yours — and you draft the section on implementation, the part the field still underrates and you have made your whole contribution. Somewhere in the writing you remember that this slow, contested, peer-reviewed inch is how the work travels beyond the rooms you can reach. A clinician helps one person at a time. You are trying to help the system that helps the people, and the price of that scale is that you almost never get to feel it directly. You made that trade with open eyes. Most Tuesdays you'd make it again.

Late afternoon, a younger colleague — someone three years into the path you were once three years into — asks for twenty minutes. You give her forty. **Mentoring** has crept up on you as one of the most quietly load-bearing verbs of the whole life; the field is small and the only way it grows the strange interdisciplinary fluency you stake everything on is if you hand it forward. She's anxious she's not doing enough, fast enough. You recognize her exactly. You tell her the truest thing you know: that the work is already happening, that she doesn't have to wait for full authority to matter, and that the discipline is to deploy as she builds. You're quoting yourself from ten years ago. It turned out to be true.

And then — because you protected this, against every optimizer who told you it was an indulgence — the evening is yours, and you **make**. Not for the program. Not for a paper. You go into the studio and you put paint to paper with no outcome attached, and the Dear Ordinary practice that you were once told to treat as a hobby reveals itself again as the thing that keeps the rest of it human. The switching between the clinical-systems mind and the making hand is not inefficiency. It is the only reason you're still here, twenty years in, instead of burned out at five. The art is the fuel. It was always the fuel.

So who do you work with? A handful of people you trust completely, scattered across time zones, most of whom now lead the work you once led. Lay facilitators and the trainers who train them. People with lived experience whose names sit first on the papers. A younger colleague or two who will outrun you, which is the point.

What are you busy with? Listening, watching, noticing, adapting, revising, asking, reading, writing, mentoring, making. Almost none of it is dramatic. Almost all of it is the patient construction of instruments that let other people do the thing in rooms you'll never enter.

Who do you help? Mostly people you'll never meet, in places that had nothing, reached by someone you trained to train the person who reached them.

And the impact? It is real, and it is mediated, and it is probabilistic, and you have made your peace with all three. You can't point to a face and say *her, I saved her*. You can point to a training system that didn't exist, that now travels, that lets evidence-based care for depression reach people who were receiving none — and you can hold, honestly, that some meaningful fraction of relief in the world this year exists because you spent your one strange combination of gifts on the bottleneck instead of the spotlight.

You close the laptop. The inlet has gone dark. The coffee cup is still on the counter from this morning, in a kitchen that hasn't changed, in a life that has — quietly, deliberately, and on purpose — become exactly the one you were building toward on the Tuesdays when it was still only a plan.
