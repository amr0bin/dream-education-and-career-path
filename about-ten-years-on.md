# About

*Written from the far side of the decade — the about page as it might honestly read on that Tuesday.*

---

I design the training systems that let people who are not specialists deliver good care for depression — with fidelity, at low cost, in places that have almost none. That sentence took me fifteen years to be able to write plainly. Everything before it was the apprenticeship; everything since has been the work.

I came to this through an unusual door: a fine art degree from Emily Carr, two years of neurobiology at Dalhousie, and more than a decade designing learning systems across higher education, research institutions, and the public sector in Canada. For years those looked like separate lives. What connected them — what I eventually built a career on — was a single stubborn skill: making knowledge travel without thinning out. Knowing where understanding actually forms in a learner, and where it quietly fails to. It turned out that skill was the missing instrument in one of the largest unmet needs in global health, and I spent this decade pointing it there.

**The pivot.** Early on, I made a hard choice that ran against my own instincts. I had been building toward a clinical practice — therapist, one room, one person at a time. I stepped off that ladder. The honest math was that a clinician helps a handful of people a week and is, to some degree, replaceable; the rarer contribution, the one almost no one else was positioned to make, was at the bottleneck *behind* the clinician. The evidence already showed that lay-delivered psychological care for depression works. What the field lacked was the craft of *training delivery at scale without degrading quality* — a learning-design and implementation problem dressed up as a clinical one. That was mine to take.

**The build.** The seed was already in my hands: Make Space, a year-long creative-health practice I'd been running, and its toolkit — the *Other Bodies* deck of fifty-two creative care cards pairing feelings with natural forms, alongside the emotional compass, the topography, and the emotion-story tools I'd developed in practice. I stopped treating these as a personal curriculum and started treating them as a clinical intervention that needed to be made transferable. Over the decade I turned them into an open-licensed facilitator training package: not the prompts themselves, but the *system that teaches someone else to deliver them faithfully* — structured around the four-second decisions that separate real holding from hollow imitation, with built-in fidelity checks, debrief protocols, and a train-the-trainer architecture so the model could grow without me at its center.

**The credentials, kept lean.** I took the credential that actually unlocked the work — a master's in global mental health at LSHTM, for the network and the legitimacy it bought inside the task-shifting world — and earned a doctorate through the implementation research itself rather than as a detour from it. I let go of the other four degrees I'd once planned. My disability-justice scholarship stayed as a lens rather than a line of credentials; it shaped every adaptation decision about who gets left out and why.

**The partnerships.** None of this is solo work, and the about page that pretends otherwise is lying. The model now runs through partnerships with district mental health programs and community organizations across three countries in East Africa and South Asia. The intervention was never imported intact — it was rebuilt locally around culturally specific idioms of distress, by teams whose lead authors' names sit first on our papers, as they should. The people I work most closely with were facilitators when I met them and are now trainers who train their own trainers. That progression — from one practitioner to a self-propagating system — is the single accomplishment I'm proudest of, and it is theirs as much as mine.

**The numbers, honestly.** Over the decade, the training system has been used to prepare several hundred lay facilitators and a smaller cohort of facilitator-trainers, across roughly a dozen delivery sites. Through them, the model has reached people experiencing depression — in the thousands, though I state that figure carefully, because the honest truth of scaled work is that the impact is mediated and estimated, not witnessed. I have never met most of the people this work has helped. I count instead what I can defend: facilitators trained, trainers multiplied, fidelity sustained across sites, an open-licensed package that other research teams have adapted without needing my permission, and a small body of peer-reviewed evidence that the multiplier holds.

**The knowledge.** I published — not prolifically, but where it mattered: the conceptual paper that framed the tools, a study of the Other Bodies instruments, a systematic review, and a run of implementation-science papers on the part of this field that remains chronically underrated — not *what works*, but *how to make what works survive contact with the real world*. I kept writing publicly throughout, in *Fieldwork* and at the Therapeutic Studio, treating the Substacks as open scholarship so the questions traveled as freely as the answers.

**The thread I refused to cut.** Through all of it I kept making art. Dear Ordinary, my figurative practice, was the one thing every optimizer told me to drop, and the one thing I knew I couldn't. The switching between the systems mind and the making hand was never inefficiency — it was the reason I lasted twenty years instead of burning out at five. The art is the fuel. It always was.

**What it adds up to.** If you want the verbs: I spent the decade listening, watching, noticing, adapting, revising, coaching, writing, mentoring, and making. Almost none of it was dramatic. Almost all of it was the patient construction of instruments that let other people do the thing in rooms I will never enter.

I cannot point to a single face and say *her, I reached her*. I can point to a training system that did not exist, that now travels, that lets evidence-based care for depression reach people who were receiving none. I made my peace, early and on purpose, with impact that is real, mediated, and probabilistic. It is the trade scale demands. Most days, I would make it again.
