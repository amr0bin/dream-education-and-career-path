Deployment — Living Document

What I can do right now
at every stage of the path.

This document answers one question: where do I have the freedom to act, even without more credentials, more authority, or more time? The answer is: more places than it feels like. This is the map of those places, organised by what is available now and what opens at each stage of training.

The principle underneath all of this
The people who have the most impact are not the ones who waited until they were fully prepared. They are the ones who deployed as they built — let the deployment inform the building, and arrived at full authority with a body of work already behind them. The depression treatment gap does not need a completed PhD before it gets attention. It needs attention now, at the level that is currently possible, with the plan to deepen and extend that contribution over twenty years. The work is already happening. The task is to treat it with the seriousness it deserves.

Everything in this tab is available right now — no new credential, no institutional affiliation, no permission required. These are the moves that cost the least and compound the most. Some are already running and need only to be named and treated seriously. Others are within reach in the next three to six months.

Already Running — Name It and Treat It Seriously
The Work That Is Already Deployment

These things are already happening. The shift required is not adding new work — it is recognising what is already underway as the beginning of a body of contribution, and treating it accordingly.

Running
Make Space — Reframe as a Community Mental Health Program
Weekly facilitation · Year-long curriculum · Original clinical tools · Already reaching people with depression

Make Space is not a personal curriculum shared publicly. It is a community mental health early intervention for depression — a year-long structured curriculum in creative health facilitation, with original conceptual tools (the Other Bodies emotional compass, star chart, topography, and emotion stories) that have no direct equivalent in the published literature. It is already reaching people. It is already doing something.

The reframe costs nothing. What changes is how it is documented, how it is described professionally, and how it is treated in applications, conversations with researchers, and eventual publications. A session log kept now becomes field notes. A participant feedback mechanism added now becomes preliminary outcome data. A reflective journal entry written after each session now becomes the raw material of a qualitative study in two years.

  • Begin keeping a brief reflective journal entry after each session — what was observed, what surprised, what the tools produced in participants
  • Add a simple optional feedback mechanism at the end of each curriculum phase — not a formal instrument, just a few open questions about experience
  • Describe Make Space in professional bios and applications as a community mental health facilitation program in creative health, not as a personal project
  • Begin using the language of creative health and depression prevention when describing the work publicly

The Other Bodies tools are original clinical instruments. They should be described that way — not as design artefacts or illustrations, but as conceptual tools for emotional mapping developed through creative health facilitation practice.

Running
Accessible Learning Labs Substack — Treat It as Public Scholarship
Weekly · Practitioner audience · Building the intellectual platform that amplifies future research

The Substack is the highest-compounding professional asset running right now. Every piece published on accessibility, disability justice, and learning design for health organisations is contributing to the intellectual infrastructure of a field that needs exactly this thinking. It is building an audience of practitioners who will eventually be the people who adopt, cite, and build on the research.

The move is to make it do double work. Each month, pick one idea from the emerging conceptual paper on learning systems and accessible depression care and develop it publicly. The Substack becomes the laboratory for the paper. The paper becomes the formalisation of what the Substack has been working out. They compound together.

  • Write one post per month that builds toward the conceptual paper — the relationship between learning system design and intervention efficacy, what accessibility means in depression treatment, why training infrastructure is the bottleneck the field has not named
  • Begin explicitly naming depression, creative health, and the global treatment gap in Substack copy and description — make the focus visible to search and to new readers
  • Cross-reference the Substack in every professional bio, application, and research introduction as evidence of sustained public intellectual engagement with the field

Three documented projects · Systems-level impact · Health sector and research institution experience

The three case studies — province-wide transition to care-based service, national research institution learning evaluation, equitable experiential learning at scale — are already evidence of systems-level implementation work. Start Now — No Prerequisites, Within Reach in Months
The Moves That Change Everything Downstream

These actions are available right now. Each one changes the quality of every application, conversation, and collaboration that follows it. None requires a credential or an institutional affiliation.

Highest priority
Write the Conceptual Paper
Three to six months · No affiliation required · Changes every application and conversation from this point forward

This is the single highest-leverage move available right now. The argument is specific and only this combination of expertise can make it: that designing the learning systems which train practitioners to deliver depression interventions is as important as designing the interventions themselves — and that accessibility must be built into both from the beginning, not retrofitted at the end.

This paper, published in a peer-reviewed journal, changes the LSHTM MSc application, the MA application, the PhD application, every grant application, and every professional introduction. It is the first move from practice into scholarship. It does not require a university. It requires the reading, the argument, and the writing.

  • Read: Cochrane reviews on psychological treatments for depression, Patel's task-shifting trials, Fancourt's 2019 Lancet paper, two or three papers from Implementation Science on mental health intervention delivery
  • Draft the argument — not a literature review, but a conceptual contribution that names the gap and proposes a framework
  • Target: Global Mental Health (Cambridge, open access) as primary; Arts in Psychotherapy or International Journal of Mental Health Systems as alternatives
  • Timeline: three months to draft, three months to submission
Cochrane Library Global Mental Health Journal Implementation Science Arts in Psychotherapy

The paper does not need to be perfect before submission. It needs to make a real argument that the field has not made. Reviewers will improve it. The act of submitting is the move.

High priority
Read the Foundational Literature
Three months · Self-directed · Transforms the quality of everything that follows

These are not background reading. They are the intellectual framework the future research will sit inside. Read them before anything else, in this order. They will change how the Substack is written, how Make Space is framed, how the conceptual paper is argued, and how every professional conversation about this work is conducted.

  • Vikram Patel — Where There Is No Psychiatrist. The most important practical text in global mental health. Task-shifting, community care, what it actually means to treat depression at scale.
  • Lancet Commission on Global Mental Health 2018 — The foundational policy document. Defines the treatment gap, sets the agenda, maps where additional effort has the most leverage.
  • Fancourt & Finn, The Lancet 2019 — The epidemiological foundation for creative health as a legitimate intervention for depression. The evidence base the creative health work will eventually contribute to.
  • WHO mhGAP Intervention Guide — The WHO protocol for non-specialist delivery of mental health interventions. Essential for designing scalable approaches that can be delivered by community practitioners.
  • Cochrane Reviews on Psychological Treatments for Depression — The most rigorous summaries of what works, for whom, and at what effect size. Read the depression reviews first.
