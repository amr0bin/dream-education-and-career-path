# About

*The broader route — the about page as it might honestly read on that Tuesday, ten years on.*

---

I am a clinician, researcher, and creative-health practitioner whose single subject is depression — how it works in the body and brain, how it answers to creative and expressive approaches, how those approaches reach the people who currently cannot access them, and how the systems that train practitioners to deliver them can be designed to scale. I work in the room with one person, and I work at the level where systems change, and over the last decade I stopped treating those as two different jobs.

I came to this by an unusual route, and the route turned out to be the point. A Bachelor of Fine Arts from Emily Carr taught me to think through making. Two years of neurobiology at Dalhousie gave me the biological substrate most practitioners in creative health don't carry. Fifteen years designing learning systems across higher education, research institutions, and the public sector taught me the rarest thing I know: how to make knowledge travel without thinning out. For years those looked like three separate lives. This decade is what happened when I pointed all of them at one problem.

**The clinical work.** I trained the long way on purpose, because I wanted to earn the room. A master's in counselling psychology, then a doctorate in clinical psychology, then registration as a psychologist in British Columbia — each step oriented from day one toward depression. I now hold a caseload built around people living with depression, using integrated expressive and arts-based approaches as part of the therapeutic work: people whose healing moves through image and material when words have gone flat, neurodivergent and disabled clients failed by systems that exclude most people before they ever reach a clinician, creatives and grieving people and those flattened by burnout. This is the ground floor of everything. I research depression credibly because I sit with it weekly. The room keeps the rest of the work honest.

**The creative-health program.** The seed was already in my hands when this decade began: Make Space, and its toolkit — the *Other Bodies* deck of fifty-two creative-care cards pairing feelings with natural forms, alongside the emotional compass, the topography, and the emotion-story tools I developed in practice. I stopped treating these as a personal curriculum shared publicly and built them into something that holds: an evidence-based community mental-health early intervention, with validated outcome measurement built in from the first cohort, a structured facilitator-training curriculum, and a train-the-trainer architecture so the model grows beyond me. It runs now through social-prescribing partnerships with health systems that refer to it directly, and through facilitator-trainers who train their own. What began as one practitioner in one room has become a system that propagates — which was always the only way creative health reaches population scale.

**The research.** I built a coherent program rather than a scatter of papers, and every piece pointed the same direction: how creative and arts-based approaches to depression can be designed, adapted, and delivered at scale, in underserved settings, through non-specialists, with accessibility at the centre. It started before I had any affiliation — a conceptual paper arguing that the training system which delivers a depression intervention is as important as the intervention itself. It deepened through a pre-registered systematic review of what the evidence actually shows, a feasibility trial of a creative intervention, and a run of implementation-science studies on the part of the field that remains chronically underrated: not *what works*, but *how to make what works survive contact with the real world*. Through the LSHTM network I built international partnerships in low-resource settings — collaborations where local leads hold first authorship and the research serves their priorities, not a Canadian dissertation. My disability-studies training from Leeds runs underneath all of it as the lens on who gets left out and why.

**The teaching.** Authority is only worth having if you hand it forward. I teach and supervise the next generation of clinician-researchers — practicum students, emerging researchers working across the same disciplinary borders I had to cross — and the academic platform gives the research reach while the research keeps the teaching real. The strange interdisciplinary fluency this work depends on only survives if more people learn to hold it.

**The public scholarship.** I kept writing in the open the whole way — at Accessible Learning Labs and the Therapeutic Studio — treating the Substacks as public scholarship so the questions travelled as freely as the answers. That writing matured into keynotes at global mental health and arts-in-health gatherings, and into longer-form work for practitioners and for people living with depression: accessible public communication about depression that the field rarely produces well.

**The art.** Through all of it I kept making. Dear Ordinary, my figurative practice, moved through its full arc of phases and was shown as work in its own right. It was never decoration and never a hobby I tolerated. It is the embodied knowing the analytical work cannot generate alone, and the mechanism by which I hold this many modes without going brittle. The switching between the clinical mind, the research mind, and the making hand is not inefficiency. It is the epistemology. It is the reason the contribution is mine and not a thinner version anyone could make.

**What it adds up to.** The verbs of the decade were: sitting, attending, holding, witnessing, listening, noticing, adapting, revising, supervising, teaching, writing, and making. I helped people directly, one room at a time, and I built systems that reached people I will never meet — a caseload and a training model and a body of evidence and a network and a public conversation, all aimed at the same target.

Most people working on depression are trained in one lane and spend their careers defending it. I built fluency across clinical practice, creative health, learning-systems design, and the research evidence, and pointed all of it at the leading cause of disability in the world — where more than three quarters of people in low-income countries still receive no care at all. That specific combination did not exist in the field when I started. Building it, and putting it to work, was the point.
