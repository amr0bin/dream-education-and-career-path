# An Average Tuesday, Ten Years On — The Whole Portfolio

*The involved path. The one where you kept the room, the credentials, the research, and the art, all at once. Written honestly — the wholeness and the cost — so you can feel it before you choose it.*

---

You wake before the alarm, same as the other life, in the same kitchen, the same grey light coming up over the inlet. You make coffee in the dark. But this Tuesday is heavier in your hands, and fuller, and you feel both of those before your feet are properly under you. There is a person coming to see you at ten. There is a person, and you said yes to the version of your life where there is always a person.

The first verb, though, is the same as the lean life would have been: **listening**. The early call is with the international team — a district program a continent away, the facilitator-trainers who were facilitators when you met them, the lead investigator whose name goes first on your papers. You listen more than you speak. The training system you built travels through them now. This part you share with the other version of yourself, the leaner one: the watching of session footage, the noticing of a four-second decision a facilitator rushed past, the patient construction of instruments for rooms you'll never enter. You do this for forty minutes. You're good at it. It scales.

And then at ten you close the laptop and a real person sits down across from you, and everything changes register.

This is the hour the other path gave up, and this is why you didn't. You **sit**. You **attend**. You **hold** a room that contains exactly one suffering human being, and you are not three degrees removed from their relief — you are the relief, or its instrument, in real time, with nowhere to hide and no system to mediate it. They are depressed in the particular, unrepeatable way that this one person is depressed, and you have spent a decade learning what the room asks for. Today it asks for paper and a little paint, because words have gone flat for them, and you watch something move across their face that you will never be able to put in a grant application. You **witness** it. The training and the neurobiology and the research literacy are all in there somewhere, underneath, but in this hour you are not a systems designer. You are a Registered Psychologist with one person in front of you, and the contact is the whole point, and it costs you something real to give it. You feel that cost in your sternum when they leave. This is the thing. This is the thing you refused to trade away.

You sit for a minute afterward, between registers, and feel the strange double exposure of your own life: the call at eight that touched thousands you'll never see, the hour at ten that touched one you'll never forget. The whole architecture of this path is the refusal to choose between them. On a good Tuesday that refusal feels like wholeness — like you are the one person in the field who knows depression from the inside of the room *and* the inside of the system *and* the inside of the making hand. On a hard Tuesday it feels like being spread across too many tables, and you have learned not to lie to yourself about which kind of Tuesday it is.

Midday you **revise** — a training module that isn't landing, the same craft the lean life was built entirely around, except here it's one stream among many and gets the hours it can get rather than all of them. Then you **supervise**: a practicum student, three years into the path you were once three years into, who is anxious she isn't doing enough fast enough. You give her forty minutes you didn't quite have. **Mentoring** has become load-bearing in a way no one warned you about; the field grows the strange interdisciplinary fluency you staked your life on only if you hand it forward. You **teach**, some terms — a seminar, the next generation of clinician-researchers — and the academic thread gives the research a platform and the platform gives the training systems reach, and you can feel, on the days you're rested enough to feel it, how each stream feeds the others exactly the way you promised yourself they would.

But here is the honest middle of this particular life, the part the leaner Tuesday doesn't contain. The switching has a cost, and the cost is real, and your own plan named it years ago and you still feel it: moving from the embodied register of the clinical room to the analytical register of research writing to the administrative register of supervision and back is not free. The transitions eat the margins. Some afternoons you finish the day having touched eight things and deepened none of them, and the fear that visits you then is the precise one you wrote into your challenges document a decade ago — that busyness is not impact, that a portfolio can feel enormously full while the depression burden it claims to address barely moves. You don't get to banish that fear. You get to keep it in the room with you as a discipline rather than a wound. On the days you're tired — and you are, sometimes, more than you expected, because the runway to all of this was long and you arrived at full deployment already carrying the fatigue of having built it — the meaning layer whispers that you could just facilitate, just make, just do the parts that love you back immediately, and let the slow invisible research drift. You notice the whisper. You don't always win against it. You forgive yourself the Tuesdays you don't.

And then, because you protected it against every optimizer and every exhausted version of yourself who wanted to let it go, the evening is **making**. Dear Ordinary. Paint to paper, no outcome attached, the practice that the lean life kept as fuel and that this life needs even more, because this life burns hotter and wider. You stand at the work and feel the registers finally quiet into one. The art is not separate from the clinical room — it was *in* the room this morning, in the paper you reached for. It is not separate from the research — it is the embodied knowing the methods can't generate alone. The switching you cursed at three o'clock reveals itself at eight as the actual mechanism, the thing that lets a person hold this much without going brittle. You were right about this part. You were right to refuse to let it go.

So who do you work with? Everyone the lean path gave you — the international team, the trainers who outgrew you, the lived-experience leads — *and* the people the lean path couldn't: the clients across the room, the students you supervise, the colleagues you teach beside. A wider circle, held less tightly, each one real.

What are you busy with? Listening, attending, holding, witnessing, noticing, adapting, revising, supervising, teaching, writing, mentoring, and making. More verbs than one life should hold. Most days you hold them anyway.

Who do you help? Both. The one across the room, whose face you'll remember, and the thousands you'll never meet, reached through the system you built. You bought direct contact and you paid for it in spread, and you knew the price going in.

And the impact? It is real, and it is mixed in kind — some of it immediate and witnessed and small, some of it vast and mediated and uncertain — and you have stopped needing it to be one clean thing. You can point to a face *and* to a training system that travels. You can also admit, on the honest Tuesdays, that you'll never be sure you allocated yourself correctly across all of it, and that no future version of you ever gets to be sure. That uncertainty is not a flaw in the plan. It is the texture of having refused to be only one thing.

You close the laptop. The inlet has gone dark. The coffee cup is still on the counter from this morning, in a kitchen that hasn't changed, in a life that has — fully, deliberately, at real cost and on purpose — become the whole thing you were building toward. Not the efficient version. The whole one. On a good Tuesday you would choose it again without pausing. On a hard one you'd pause — and then, most likely, choose it anyway, because the person who came at ten is somewhere out in the dark city tonight a little less alone, and you were *there* for that, in the room, with nothing in between.
