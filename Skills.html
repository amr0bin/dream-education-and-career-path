Skills — Living Document

The competencies that make
this work possible.

A portfolio career at the intersection of learning design, global mental health, creative health, and disability equity demands a specific constellation of skills. This document names them precisely, grounds them in the scholarship and practice of others who have built similar expertise, and identifies pathways for building and demonstrating each one.

Why this map matters
Skills are not ornamental. They are the mechanism by which intention becomes contribution. Naming them clearly — what they are, what competence actually looks like, and how to develop them deliberately — is the difference between a career that aspires and one that builds. This document is part of the working infrastructure of a twenty-year plan.

The foundation of this portfolio career rests on fifteen years of learning design expertise, now being actively reoriented toward health systems. These are the skills that are already strong, already deployable, and already generating value — and that deepen significantly as the health sector work develops.

Competency Domain 01
Learning Experience Design & Instructional Architecture

The ability to design learning systems that are coherent, evidence-informed, and responsive to how people actually acquire and transfer knowledge.

Core
Instructional Systems Design — Applied to Complex Domains
Designing learning sequences that build genuine competence in practitioners, not just knowledge recall

Competence in this area means designing learning that moves people from novice to proficient in domains that cannot be reduced to checklists. In health and mental health contexts, this requires integrating cognitive load theory, situated learning principles, and deliberate practice frameworks. The work is architectural: understanding how knowledge builds, where misconceptions form, and what conditions enable genuine skill transfer.

In the context of this career, the application is designing training systems for mental health practitioners in low-resource settings — task-shifting programs, community facilitator training, and scalable practitioner development in creative health approaches to depression.

Scholars and Practitioners
John Sweller — Cognitive load theory, foundational to all serious learning design. His work on intrinsic, extraneous, and germane load directly informs how practitioner training is sequenced for complex skills like facilitation and therapeutic technique.
Ruth Clark & Richard Mayer — Evidence-based multimedia learning principles, relevant for designing accessible digital learning in global mental health contexts where bandwidth and literacy vary.
Viviane Robinson — Educational leadership and professional learning in complex institutional environments; her work on problem-centred professional development maps directly to health authority L&D.
Learning Pathways
Formal — Short Course
ATD Certificate in Instructional Design
Self-paced professional credential. Formalises existing expertise and adds health-sector credibility to the consulting practice.
Experiential — Self-Led
Design & document a practitioner training program for Make Space facilitators
Build the full training architecture: learning outcomes, sequenced curriculum, competency indicators, and a fidelity checklist. Produces a publishable training manual.
Formal — Reading
Sweller, van Merriënboer & Paas (1998) — Cognitive Architecture and Instructional Design, Educational Psychology Review
The primary source. Read alongside real design work to test which principles hold in the health sector context.
Experiential — Consulting
Conduct an instructional audit of an existing health authority training program
Pro-bono or paid: map an existing program against evidence-based design principles. The audit report demonstrates applied expertise and produces a portfolio artefact.

The benchmark for high competence here is the ability to take a clinical or health practice that currently exists only in the heads of senior practitioners and build the training system that makes it teachable, scalable, and evaluable. That is the specific skill this career requires.

Core
Knowledge Translation — From Evidence to Practice
Making research findings available and actionable for practitioners who did not produce them

Knowledge translation is not summarising papers. It is the disciplined work of understanding what a body of evidence actually shows, identifying the gap between evidence and current practice, and designing the specific intervention — a tool, a training, a protocol, a decision support system — that closes that gap in a real context with real constraints.

In this career, knowledge translation operates at two levels: translating depression treatment research into accessible creative health programs for communities, and translating that program knowledge into training systems that enable other practitioners to deliver those programs with fidelity.

Scholars and Practitioners
Ian Graham et al. — The Knowledge-to-Action framework, widely used in health systems. Their 2006 paper in Journal of Continuing Education in the Health Professions is the primary theoretical source for knowledge translation in health contexts.
Trisha Greenhalgh — Diffusion of Innovations in health services. Her work on why evidence does or does not travel in complex systems is essential for anyone designing scalable mental health training.
The Campbell Collaboration — Practitioner knowledge translation in social care and education; a useful complement to the health-focused KT literature for work that crosses sectors.
Learning Pathways
Formal — Short Course
Knowledge Translation Canada — KT Online Course
Free, health-focused, and directly applicable. Builds the vocabulary and frameworks used by health authority collaborators.
Experiential — Self-Led
Write one Substack piece per month translating a depression research paper for practitioners
Disciplines the skill, builds the audience, and produces the intellectual track record that precedes peer-reviewed publication.
Core
Universal Design for Learning — Accessibility-Grounded Design
Designing learning that does not require adaptation because it was built for the full range of human variation from the start

Universal Design for Learning is not an add-on. It is a design philosophy grounded in the neuroscience of variability — the recognition that there is no average learner, and that systems designed for an imagined average exclude most people. In mental health contexts, this intersects directly with the depression-disability relationship: disabled people experience depression at significantly higher rates and are simultaneously the most excluded from existing mental health systems.

Competence in UDL means being able to design learning experiences that provide multiple means of representation, action and expression, and engagement — not as a compliance gesture, but as a genuine expansion of who can access and benefit from the learning.

Scholars and Practitioners
David Rose, Anne Meyer & the CAST team — Founders of UDL. The UDL Guidelines (CAST, 2018) are the primary framework. Rose and Meyer's 2002 book Teaching Every Student in the Digital Age remains the most accessible introduction.
Thomas Hehir — Disability studies and UDL in institutional contexts. His work on ableism in education provides the equity critique that prevents UDL from becoming a technical exercise without political substance.
Learning Pathways
Formal — Credential
CPACC — Certified Professional in Accessibility Core Competencies (IAAP)
The professional credential that formalises accessibility expertise for institutions and consulting clients. Already on the plan — this is the exam preparation.
Experiential — Self-Led
Conduct a UDL audit of Make Space curriculum and rebuild one module to full UDL compliance
Applied, documentable, and produces a portfolio artefact that demonstrates the skill concretely to health authority clients.
Competency Domain 02
Program Design, Facilitation & Community-Based Practice

The capacity to design, run, and evaluate structured group programs in community settings — the operational heart of Make Space and the practitioner training work.

Applied
Facilitation Design — Therapeutic and Psychoeducational Groups
Structuring and running groups that produce genuine psychological benefit rather than social activity

Facilitation in therapeutic contexts requires a different skill set from workshop facilitation or classroom instruction. The facilitator holds the emotional container of the group, tracks the wellbeing of individual members while maintaining group coherence, works with material that can activate distress, and makes real-time decisions about depth, pacing, and safety. These competencies are developed through practice, supervision, and reflective documentation — not primarily through coursework.

For this career, facilitation competence is directly deployable through Make Space, and serves as the foundation for the practitioner training work: it is not possible to build a credible facilitator training program without having done the facilitation oneself with sufficient depth to know what the skill actually requires.

Scholars and Practitioners
Irvin Yalom — The primary theoretical foundation for therapeutic group work. His Theory and Practice of Group Psychotherapy (5th ed., 2005) identifies the curative factors in group settings — instillation of hope, universality, altruism, cohesiveness — that are directly observable in Make Space sessions and provide the conceptual vocabulary for documenting outcomes.
Stephen Brookfield — Critical facilitation in adult learning. His work on the facilitation of critical reflection, particularly The Skillful Teacher and Becoming a Critically Reflective Teacher, provides the pedagogical grounding for facilitation that goes beyond content delivery to genuine transformation.
Learning Pathways
Experiential — Ongoing
Keep a reflective facilitation journal after every Make Space session
Document what was observed, what group dynamics emerged, what clinical decisions were made and why. This is the raw material of both personal skill development and eventual publication.
Formal — Short Course
Mental Health Commission of Canada — Applied Suicide Intervention Skills Training (ASIST)
Essential safety competency for anyone facilitating groups with people experiencing depression. Two-day in-person workshop, widely available in Vancouver.
Informal — Peer
Establish a peer supervision relationship with another facilitator
Monthly structured reflection on facilitation practice. Builds critical distance from one's own work and models the supervision structure that will eventually be part of the facilitator training program.
Experiential — Portfolio
Write and publish a detailed facilitation guide for one Make Space module
The guide makes implicit facilitation knowledge explicit — a prerequisite for scalable training. Published via Substack or as a standalone document, it is immediately shareable with collaborators and health authority partners.

The research skills in this portfolio serve a specific purpose: generating credible evidence about what works in creative health and depression, and contributing to the literature that shapes how mental health systems are designed. These skills are being built deliberately and in sequence, beginning now.

Competency Domain 03
Research Literacy & Epidemiological Thinking

The ability to read, evaluate, and generate health research — a prerequisite for both the LSHTM MSc and for contributing meaningfully to the depression and creative health evidence base.

Research
Critical Appraisal of Health Research
Evaluating the strength, relevance, and limits of evidence — the core intellectual skill of evidence-based practice

Critical appraisal means being able to read a paper in a health journal and determine: what kind of study is this, what can it actually establish, what are its methodological limitations, and how does it fit within the broader body of evidence on this question? Without this skill, a researcher is dependent on others' summaries and cannot evaluate whether a claimed intervention effect is real, replicable, or relevant to their population.

For this career, the primary application is evaluating the evidence base for creative health interventions in depression — a field where enthusiasm often runs ahead of rigorous evidence. Being able to name what the evidence actually shows, and what it does not, is a form of intellectual integrity that distinguishes credible contribution from advocacy.

Scholars and Practitioners
Trisha Greenhalgh — How to Read a Paper: The Basics of Evidence-Based Medicine and Healthcare (6th ed., 2019). The single most useful book for developing critical appraisal skills as a non-physician health professional. Clear, opinionated, and grounded in real research practice.
The GRADE Working Group — The international standard for rating the quality of evidence and strength of recommendations in clinical practice guidelines. Fluency with GRADE reasoning is expected in health research contexts and demonstrates methodological sophistication to collaborators.
Learning Pathways
Formal — Short Course
LSHTM: Epidemiology for Public Health (online)
First in the LSHTM sequence. Builds the foundational vocabulary for reading health research — study designs, confounding, bias, measures of association.
Experiential — Self-Led
Complete a structured critical appraisal of five creative health and depression RCTs
Using a standard CASP checklist, appraise five trials. Write a 500-word critical summary of each. The five summaries together form the foundation of a future systematic review or scoping review.
Formal — Reading
Cochrane Collaboration training modules — Understanding Evidence in Systematic Reviews
Free online. Provides fluency with the Cochrane framework used by health authority partners and the LSHTM research community.
Experiential — Portfolio
Write a Substack series: "What the Evidence Actually Says About Creative Health and Depression"
Six-part series applying critical appraisal skills publicly. Builds intellectual credibility, attracts a research-adjacent audience, and disciplines the appraisal practice.
Research
Qualitative Research Methods — Applied to Creative Health Practice
Generating rich, credible evidence about lived experience, process, and meaning — the methods that fit the research questions this career raises

Qualitative research methods — phenomenological inquiry, thematic analysis, narrative research, grounded theory — are the appropriate methodology for the earliest research questions in this career. What is the experience of depression for people who participate in creative health programs? What meanings do participants make of the work? What theoretical model best explains the mechanism of change? These are not questions that randomised controlled trials are designed to answer.

Competence in qualitative methods means understanding not just the procedures — coding, theming, member-checking — but the epistemological commitments underneath them: what counts as evidence, how trustworthiness is established, and how qualitative findings contribute to cumulative knowledge. It also means being able to defend qualitative methodology to audiences trained primarily in quantitative health research — a real skill in interdisciplinary settings.

Scholars and Practitioners
Virginia Braun & Victoria Clarke — Thematic analysis is the most widely used qualitative method in applied health research. Their 2006 paper in Qualitative Research in Psychology and their 2022 book Thematic Analysis: A Practical Guide are the primary methodological sources. Their reflexive thematic analysis framework is well-suited to the Make Space data.
Norman Denzin & Yvonna Lincoln — The SAGE Handbook of Qualitative Research is the field's primary reference. The chapters on epistemology and on defending qualitative inquiry are essential for navigating the methodological debates that arise when this research meets health research gatekeepers.
Cheryl Mattingly — Narrative reasoning in clinical practice; her work connects qualitative methods to therapeutic practice in ways that are directly relevant to creative health research design.
Learning Pathways
Formal — Short Course
Coursera: Qualitative Research Methods (University of Amsterdam)
Free audit available. Structured, internationally credible, and immediately applicable to the Make Space data already being collected.
Experiential — Self-Led
Conduct a thematic analysis of Make Space participant feedback and write it up as a draft paper
Apply Braun and Clarke's reflexive TA procedure to existing feedback data. The draft paper becomes the first submission-ready research document — and demonstrates applied qualitative competence to future graduate supervisors.

The qualitative paper from Make Space is not a future aspiration — it is possible now, with data already in hand. Completing it before the Leeds MSc application demonstrates research initiative and produces the first line of the publication record.

Research
Academic Writing — Peer-Reviewed Publication
Writing for the scholarly audience that makes research travel — the form that gives findings the authority to influence practice and policy

Academic writing is a discipline distinct from all other forms of professional writing. It demands precision in claim-making, rigorous citation of sources, adherence to field-specific conventions, and the ability to situate one's own work within the existing literature without overstating its contribution. For someone with fifteen years of professional writing experience, the adaptation to academic conventions is achievable — but it requires deliberate practice and, ideally, feedback from someone who publishes in the target journals.

The first peer-reviewed paper from Make Space is achievable without a graduate affiliation. Qualitative papers in community mental health and creative health journals do not require institutional affiliation as a condition of submission. The work is already happening. The writing is the next step.

Scholars and Practitioners
Wendy Laura Belcher — Writing Your Journal Article in Twelve Weeks (2nd ed., 2019) is the most practically useful guide to academic writing and publication for people entering the scholarly publishing system without institutional support. It is a workbook, not a style guide, and it produces a submitted paper if followed.
Paul Silvia — How to Write a Lot: A Practical Guide to Productive Academic Writing. A short, honest book about the behavioural discipline of writing regularly — essential for someone managing a portfolio career alongside a writing practice.
Learning Pathways
Formal — Self-Paced
Work through Wendy Laura Belcher's twelve-week program with the Make Space qualitative paper as the target manuscript
The program produces a submission-ready manuscript. This is the most direct path to a first publication.
Experiential — Portfolio
Submit a conceptual paper to a creative health or community mental health journal
Target journals: Arts in Psychotherapy, International Journal of Mental Health Promotion, Health Promotion International. A conceptual paper on the theoretical model underlying Make Space does not require empirical data.

The clinical and creative skills in this portfolio are the ones that cannot be acquired quickly. They develop through sustained practice, supervision, and the particular kind of knowledge that comes only from being in the room with people in distress. These are the skills being built now, through Make Space, and that will deepen significantly through the MA and clinical training.

Competency Domain 04
Creative Health & Expressive Arts Practice

The integration of arts-based methods with therapeutic intent — the distinctive intersection that no other combination of credentials and experience fully provides.

Clinical
Creative Health Facilitation — Depression-Specific Approaches
Using creative and expressive modalities as structured therapeutic intervention for people experiencing depression

Creative health facilitation for depression is not art therapy and not art-making for wellbeing in a general sense. It is the structured application of creative processes — drawing, writing, image-making, embodied practice — as a vehicle for the emotional processing, cognitive reframing, and interpersonal connection that depression disrupts. Competence requires understanding both the mechanisms of depression and the mechanisms by which creative engagement can work on those mechanisms.

The Make Space program embodies this competence in operational form. The Other Bodies tools — the emotional compass, the star chart, the topography, the emotion stories — are original clinical instruments developed through sustained facilitation practice. They are not borrowed from existing frameworks. This is the evidence of genuine expertise, not just familiarity with the field.

Scholars and Practitioners
Daisy Fancourt & Saoirse Finn — What is the Evidence on the Role of the Arts in Improving Health and Well-being? (WHO Europe, 2019). The most comprehensive review of arts and health evidence to date. Provides the evidentiary framing within which Make Space should be positioned.
Shaun McNiff — Art as Medicine and Art-Based Research. McNiff is the most rigorous theorist of arts-based practice as therapeutic intervention, and his concept of artistic inquiry as a form of knowing is directly relevant to the Dear Ordinary practice as epistemological infrastructure.
Cathy Malchiodi — Handbook of Art Therapy and Trauma and Expressive Arts Therapy. The most clinically grounded practitioner in the expressive arts field. Her integration of neuroscience and arts-based practice provides the biological mechanism that connects creative facilitation to depression outcomes.
Learning Pathways
Formal — Certificate
Vancouver Art Therapy Institute — Introduction to Art Therapy Certificate
A local, accessible entry point that provides the clinical vocabulary and basic assessment framework for arts-based therapeutic work before the full art therapy credential.
Experiential — Self-Led
Develop and document the theoretical model underlying the Other Bodies tools
Write a 3,000-word theoretical paper explaining why these tools work — what mechanisms they engage, what the evidence suggests about those mechanisms, how they connect to the broader literature. This is the conceptual foundation for the first publication.
Formal — Reading
WHO Europe Report: Fancourt & Finn (2019)
Read in full, with annotation. Map every Make Space activity to the evidence categories in this report. The mapping exercise becomes the literature review for the first paper.
Experiential — Portfolio
Present the Make Space model at a community mental health or arts in health conference
Conference presentations are available before peer-reviewed publication and begin building the field network. Target: BC Art Therapy Association symposium, Canadian Arts Health Alliance events.
Clinical
Depression — Theoretical and Clinical Literacy
Deep understanding of what depression is, how it presents, what the evidence supports, and what people experiencing it actually need

Depression literacy at the level required for this career goes beyond general mental health awareness. It encompasses the current diagnostic and theoretical frameworks for depression across the DSM and ICD systems; the neurobiology of depression as it relates to anhedonia, rumination, and behavioural withdrawal; the evidence base for first-line treatments (CBT, behavioural activation, interpersonal therapy, pharmacotherapy) and their mechanisms; the chronic and recurrent nature of depressive episodes; and the specific ways depression intersects with disability, neurodivergence, cultural context, and social determinants.

This literacy enables accurate identification of who Make Space is and is not appropriate for, honest communication about what the program can and cannot do, and the ability to engage credibly with clinical collaborators and researchers who have formal training.

Scholars and Practitioners
Andrew Solomon — The Noonday Demon: An Atlas of Depression. While not a clinical text, this Pulitzer Prize-winning work provides the most comprehensive account of depression as lived experience, treatment landscape, and social phenomenon. Essential for developing the empathic imagination that creative health facilitation requires alongside clinical knowledge.
Jonathan Rottenberg — The Depths: The Evolutionary Origins of the Age of Depression. An evolutionary and psychological account of depression that challenges conventional clinical framings — important for developing the theoretical flexibility that original clinical work requires.
Ricardo Araya — Depression in low-resource settings and task-shifting models of treatment at scale. His work at LSHTM and the evidence from Chile's Tratamiento con Apoyo de Proveedores de Salud No Especializados (TAPS) program is the foundational evidence for scalable depression care — the model this career aims to extend through creative approaches.
Learning Pathways
Formal — Short Course
LSHTM: Global Mental Health (online short course)
Third in the LSHTM sequence. Places depression within the global treatment gap, task-shifting models, and low-resource settings that this career is aimed at.
Experiential — Self-Led
Conduct and document safety screening for every Make Space cohort using a validated depression screener
Use the PHQ-9 as a pre-program screen (with appropriate referral pathways). This builds clinical literacy, produces preliminary outcome data, and demonstrates responsible program design to health authority partners.

Competency Domain 05
Health Systems Literacy & Global Mental Health

Understanding how mental health systems are structured, resourced, and constrained — at local and global scales — as the precondition for designing interventions that can actually travel.

Applied
Health Systems Thinking — Mental Health Delivery Architecture
Understanding how mental health services are structured, funded, and constrained at the system level

Systems thinking in health means understanding that individual interventions do not exist outside of the organisational, financial, and political structures in which they are delivered. A depression program that works in a well-resourced Canadian health authority setting may fail completely in a low-resource context — not because the clinical model is wrong, but because the implementation assumptions were built into a specific system architecture. Competence in health systems thinking means being able to read a system, identify the leverage points, and design accordingly.

For this career, the most relevant application is understanding the task-shifting model: how WHO and global health researchers have developed frameworks for training non-specialist health workers to deliver evidence-based mental health interventions, what fidelity and supervision requirements this imposes, and how training systems can be designed to make this work at scale.

Scholars and Practitioners
Vikram Patel — The field's foremost advocate for task-shifting and the global mental health treatment gap. His 2018 TED Talk and his work with Sangath in India are the most accessible entry point. His paper "Mental health for all by involving all" (2013, The Lancet) is the primary reference for the task-shifting model that underlies the scale ambition of this career.
WHO mhGAP (Mental Health Gap Action Programme) — The international framework for scaling mental health care in low- and middle-income countries. Fluency with mhGAP is expected by global health collaborators and funders, and the training design principles it encodes are directly relevant to Make Space facilitator training.
Mary De Silva — Implementation science in global mental health. Her work on the scaling of mental health interventions provides the methodological framework for moving from a pilot program to a replicable model.
Learning Pathways
Formal — Reading
WHO mhGAP Intervention Guide — read in full
The primary international framework. Map the Make Space program against mhGAP principles to identify where it aligns, where it extends, and where it diverges — and why.
Formal — Short Course
Harvard Global Mental Health: Trauma & Recovery (part-time, optional)
Builds the field network in global mental health. Participants from low- and middle-income countries become the first international professional relationships for the research program.
Experiential — Self-Led
Map the Make Space facilitator training model against WHO task-shifting principles
Write a two-page brief describing how Make Space facilitator training could operate as a task-shifting model for creative health approaches to depression. This document becomes the concept paper for a future grant application.
Experiential — Portfolio
Apply for one health sector informational interview per quarter at a BC health authority L&D department
Build the vocabulary, the relationships, and the systemic context that shapes everything downstream. The conversations also clarify where learning design expertise is most needed and least available in the health sector.
Competency Domain 06
Disability Justice & Equity-Centred Design

The frameworks that ensure this work does not reproduce the exclusions it is designed to address — and that position the consulting practice as a genuine specialist in accessible health system design.

Applied
Disability Justice Framework — Applied to Mental Health Systems
Understanding disability as a social and political reality, not a medical category — and designing mental health systems accordingly

Disability justice is the framework that emerged from the disability rights movement and that centres the most marginalised disabled people — people of colour, trans and queer disabled people, people in poverty — in the analysis of what equity actually requires. It is distinct from the medical model of disability (which locates the problem in the individual's impairment), the social model of disability (which locates the problem in societal barriers), and even mainstream accessibility compliance (which often satisfies the letter of equity without its substance).

For this career, disability justice is not a philosophical add-on. Disabled people experience depression at significantly higher rates than the general population, and they are simultaneously the most excluded from existing mental health systems. Any serious attempt to address the treatment gap must grapple with this intersection directly — not as a special case, but as the condition that reveals what genuine accessibility actually requires.

Scholars and Practitioners
Mia Mingus — One of the founders of the disability justice framework. Her essays, including "Access Intimacy: The Missing Link" and "Changing the Framework: Disability Justice," articulate the political and relational dimensions of access that compliance frameworks miss entirely.
Leah Lakshmi Piepzna-Samarasinha — Care Work: Dreaming Disability Justice (2018). The most accessible book-length introduction to disability justice practice, grounded in direct experience and community organising.
Sins Invalid — The disability justice performance project that produced the foundational principles of the framework. Their Skin, Tooth, and Bone is the primary source document for the ten principles of disability justice.
Learning Pathways
Formal — MSc
Leeds MSc Disability Studies — the academic formation of this expertise
The three-year part-time program that builds the theoretical and research grounding for the disability-depression intersection. Already on the plan. The consulting practice grows directly from the curriculum.
Experiential — Consulting
Design and offer a disability-informed accessibility review for one health or research organisation
Apply disability justice principles to a real organisation's learning or service design. Write up the case study (with permission). This becomes the first portfolio piece for Accessible Learning Labs consulting.

The consulting practice — Accessible Learning Labs — derives its premium positioning directly from this expertise. Most accessibility consultants work from compliance frameworks. Grounding the practice in disability justice, and demonstrating that through published work and graduate credentials, is the differentiator that commands higher rates and more meaningful institutional partnerships.

Applied
Public Writing & Intellectual Platform Building
Making ideas travel in the space between academic publication and mass media — the form that builds field influence before and alongside formal credentials

Public intellectual writing — rigorous, accessible, and grounded in genuine expertise — is a distinct skill from both academic writing and professional communication. It requires the ability to take a complex idea, establish its importance for a general educated reader, develop it with care and nuance, and land it in a form that is genuinely useful. The Substack is the vehicle for this in this career. Written well and consistently, it becomes the intellectual infrastructure that makes everything else more legible — to collaborators, funders, health authority partners, graduate programme supervisors, and the audience that eventually encounters the research.

The benchmark is not popularity. It is the capacity to articulate the ideas clearly enough that the people who need to understand them can, and to do so with enough rigour that the writing does not misrepresent the evidence or overstate the conclusions. That standard is both ethical and strategic: it is what distinguishes a serious contributor from an enthusiast.

Scholars and Practitioners
Helen Sword — Stylish Academic Writing (2012) and Air & Light & Time & Space (2017). Sword's research on what makes academic prose readable provides the bridge between scholarly rigor and public accessibility. Her "writer's diet" test is a practical editing tool.
Ed Yong — Science journalist and Pulitzer Prize winner (An Immense World, I Contain Multitudes). Not a scholar but an exemplar of what rigorous, careful, accessible writing about complex science looks like at its highest level. A useful stylistic benchmark.
Learning Pathways
Experiential — Ongoing
Publish one Substack piece per month — sustained, for twelve consecutive months
Discipline and consistency over performance and virality. The twelve pieces together form a body of work that demonstrates sustained intellectual engagement with the field. They also form the raw material for a book proposal in year three or four.
Experiential — Self-Led
Pitch and write one piece for a health-sector or disability-sector publication outside the Substack
Target publications: The Tyee, Healthy Debate, Mad in America, The Disability Visibility Project. An accepted piece in an established publication expands the audience and demonstrates that the writing meets an editorial standard.

The principle beneath the skills

Competence in each of these domains matters. But the rarest and highest-value skill in this entire constellation is the capacity to hold all of them at once — to be in the room with a person experiencing depression and simultaneously thinking about the population-level evidence, the training system that would make this replicable, the disability justice analysis of who is currently excluded, and the research question that would make this knowledge travel. That integration is what makes this career singular. The individual skills are learnable. The integration is the work of twenty years.

Reading this matrix honestly

The gaps in this matrix are not weaknesses to be embarrassed about. They are the map of the work ahead — the specific places where deliberate investment, sustained practice, and formal training will produce the most return. A career that can name its own gaps is a career that is actually building, not just performing confidence. The discipline is not to close every gap immediately but to close the right gaps in the right order — beginning with the ones that unlock everything else.
